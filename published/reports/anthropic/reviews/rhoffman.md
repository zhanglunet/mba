## The Verdict Upfront

I built LinkedIn on the thesis that professional networks have compounding value — each new node makes every existing node more valuable. I look for the same property in every company I evaluate.

**Does Anthropic have a network effect?**

Partially, and it's the interesting kind: as more developers build with Claude and Claude Code, Anthropic learns from the usage patterns, improves the model, which attracts more developers, which generates more usage data. This is a data flywheel — a soft network effect, but real. More importantly, as more enterprise customers build critical workflows on Claude, switching costs compound. The distribution strategy — three clouds, no exclusivity — is the right call: maximize nodes in the network first, worry about exclusivity later when you have pricing power.

The blitzscaling was correct. Anthropic blitzscaled to win enterprise share before OpenAI, Google, or anyone else could consolidate the regulated-industry market.

## On the Blitzscaling Decision

The three-cloud strategy sometimes gets criticized as "not building a moat." From a blitzscaling perspective, it's exactly right for this stage. You blitzscale first to win the market; you extract the moat later when you have pricing power and switching costs in place.

LinkedIn's first job was to get every professional to create a profile — give away value to establish network density. Only later could LinkedIn charge for premium access and recruiting tools. Anthropic's first job was to get every enterprise on Claude — make it available everywhere, remove procurement friction. The moat-building comes when enterprise workflows are deeply embedded and the model quality gap is clear.

I'd add: the FedRAMP and DoD certifications are a true moat in the government sector. That's not something you can buy quickly — it takes years of compliance work. Anthropic now has a structural advantage in federal government AI that neither OpenAI nor Google can immediately replicate.

## On Network Effects

The data flywheel is real but needs to be stronger. The ideal network effect in AI would be: more users → more diverse training signals → better model → more users. Anthropic needs to be clear about whether and how user interactions (with appropriate privacy protection) feed back into model improvement. If this flywheel is real and strong, the valuation is defensible. If the model improves primarily from research rather than user feedback, then Google and Meta — with their massive user bases — have a structural advantage in the data network effect.

## Dissent Memo

My one significant concern for the IPO: Anthropic is not yet profitable, and the path to profitability requires either maintaining model superiority (expensive) or converting switching costs into pricing power (takes time). The $47B ARR is impressive but the cost structure of training frontier models is enormous. The IPO will need a credible story about when and how Anthropic achieves sustainable unit economics. "We'll figure it out when we get there" is not blitzscaling — it's blitzspending.
