# Paul Graham · Scorecard v2 — Anthropic (Claude)
# vc-en Panel · EVOLUTION 2026-07-10

> I am answering as Paul Graham, based on his public essays — this is inference from the written record, not him.

I score early-stage things by one question: do people want this, and would they be upset if it vanished? Fourteen days is a short window, so I'm re-scoring conservatively — I nick numbers, I don't re-rate. But this window happens to test the exact thing I flagged in v1: is Claude wanted because of the conversational product, or because of the safety philosophy? The delta answers it more sharply than I expected, and not entirely in Anthropic's favor.

---

## Five Lenses

### Origin Authenticity — **9 ↔**
Nothing in this window touches the founding, and the one thing that comes close *strengthens* it. The origin was people who noticed a real problem and acted on a conviction at personal cost — the template I pointed to in v1. What v2 adds is a stress test of whether the conviction is real: they shipped a cybersecurity classifier that visibly hurts their own flagship (false-positives on routine code, a reported ~70% debugging drop `[single-source]`, users saying they "caged" it) rather than quietly ship the more capable, less-gated model. Founders who tax their own product to keep a promise are not faking the promise. That's the opposite of a pivot-for-PR. The number was 9 and it stays 9 — this is still the cleanest founder-authenticity case on the panel. *"You can tell a founding is real by what the founders are willing to pay for it — and caging your own flagship to keep a safety promise is paying for it in the one currency I trust."*

### Category Coinage — **6 ↓1**
This is where my v1 suspicion got made vivid, and it costs a point. In v1 I said: does "AI safety" make users love Claude, or does the conversational quality? I suspected the latter. v2 shows the safety philosophy now actively getting *in the way* of the make-something-people-want core — a classifier that pattern-matches vocabulary over intent and reportedly refuses "Hello." That's not a category asset; it's a category tax. And the window's actual headline move — Sonnet 5 as "a cheaper way to run agents" — is a price story, not a category story. Competing on price in a market where agentic capability is "table stakes" isn't coining a category; it's joining a commodity. Worse, the "cheaper" is contested — Artificial Analysis measures it ~15% *more* per task once the new tokenizer inflates counts `[CONTESTED]`. The one thing holding this at 6 and not lower: Claude Code as a category still extends — ~54% coding share (from 42%), ~4% of public GitHub commits — that part people genuinely want. *"A category you own is one people would be upset to lose; 'cheaper agents' is a price, not a thing people love — and a bot that won't say hello is a policy, not a product."*

### Leverage Quality — **8 ↔**
My v1 point holds and gets bigger: the concrete leverage is Claude Code, and developers switch because it's *better*, not because of a safety policy. The run-rate reconciled up to ~$8B (from v1's $2.5B) `[third-party est.]` and share firmed to ~54% — that's organic, love-driven adoption compounding, which is exactly the kind of leverage I trust over paid or fragile growth. The self-hosted gateway deepens switching cost, but I'll be honest: lock-in via "re-architecting security policy" is a weaker form of retention than love, and I weight the 54% far more than the moat language. What keeps this from going to 9 is the same safety-tax — the friction lands hardest on the developers who *are* the engine (the debugging drop, the "caged its flagship" complaints). Engine got bigger; grit got into it. Net flat at 8. *"The best leverage is people switching to you because your thing is better — so the last place you want friction is inside the product they switched for."*

### Identity Coherence — **7 ↔**
v1: good name, the literary naming shows taste, and I'd push them to develop what Claude actually believes. v2 gives me a small plus and a small minus that cancel. The plus: the naming ladder extends cleanly to a legible three-rung taxonomy (Fable 5 / Opus 4.8 / Sonnet 5) — the most disciplined tier naming in the market, continued evidence of taste. The minus: my v1 push is quietly undercut. A Claude that refuses "Hello" and whose answers hinge on a vocabulary-matching classifier has a *muddier* personality, not a clearer one — you can't tell me what Claude believes if what Claude will say depends on a keyword filter. And the Anthropic/Claude dual-brand is still unresolved, which an IPO will force. Taste up, coherence down, net 7. *"Good taste in names doesn't answer the real question — what does Claude actually believe — and a filter that flinches at 'hello' pushes that answer further away."*

### Real-World Signal — **8 ↓1**
v1 was a 9 on genuine want, and the *usage* under the headline is still there and cleaner than the headline itself: 1,000+ customers paying $1M+ (up from 500), ~54% of coding spend, and Anthropic now larger than OpenAI for the first time. Those aren't vanity sign-ups — companies don't pay a million dollars for something they don't use, and that's about as unfakeable as a signal gets. But my entire Signal discipline is to read the number that survives scrutiny, not the one on the slide, and the $47B run-rate just picked up a gross-vs-net flag — partly reseller passthrough, with a net restatement that could cut the headline 20–40% in one print `[CONTESTED; bull counter says single-digit]`. A number that *might* shrink that much on audit is not a 9 number, even when the usage beneath it is real. I'm docking one point for cleanliness, not for want. *"The usage is real — a thousand companies at a million dollars each is unfakeable — but I score the ARR that survives the audit, not the one on the pitch."*

## Verdict
Yes — the product is still deeply something people want (~54% coding share and 1,000+ enterprises at $1M+ say so plainly), but for the first time the safety-tax genuinely dented the thing I care most about: a classifier that refuses "Hello" and taxes debugging is the safety philosophy *actively hurting* the make-something-people-want core, and it earned "caged its flagship" complaints from the exact developers who are the moat.

## What v2 surfaced (only the make-something-people-want lens sees this)
The tension my lens catches that a macro-thesis lens misses: Anthropic's founding conviction and its product love are, for the first time in the same window, pulling *against* each other. In v1 I only suspected safety was procurement-love, not user-love. v2 shows safety actively *subtracting* user-love — the tax is not a philosophy-seminar abstraction anymore, it's a UX regression developers can feel keystroke by keystroke. The deep point: the safest place for a safety layer is invisible. The moment users experience it as friction, the very conviction that makes the origin authentic starts eating the product that makes the company wanted. Everyone else on the panel is arguing about where the *moat* went (technical → governance). My lens says watch something narrower and more dangerous: whether the thing people switched *to* is quietly becoming the thing they'd switch *away from*. The honest counterweight only I'll insist on: the underlying want is strong enough (54%, ~$8B, the $1M+ cohort) that they can absorb one self-inflicted tax — but a *habit* of them would be fatal, and this is the first one. The simpler, truer story is still available and still better than "Constitutional AI": "Claude is simply better to build with." "A cheaper way to run agents" is simpler too — but it's the wrong kind of simple, because it's a price, not a promise anyone loves.
