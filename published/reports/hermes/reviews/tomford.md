## Judge Background

Tom Ford is one of the most influential designer-executives in modern luxury history. As Creative Director at Gucci (1994–2004) and Yves Saint Laurent (1999–2004) under the Gucci Group, he engineered one of the most dramatic brand revivals in luxury history, transforming a near-bankrupt Gucci into a global luxury powerhouse. He then built his eponymous brand Tom Ford (founded 2005), which he sold to Estée Lauder in 2023. His perspective is uniquely bifurcated: he is simultaneously a luxury creative (who understands the power of seduction and visual language) and a luxury CEO (who understands the business architecture behind brand value). He has spoken publicly about his admiration for Hermès's discipline and its immunity to trends.

## Overall Commentary

When I was rebuilding Gucci in the 1990s, the question I asked myself constantly was: what is desire, and how do you manufacture it? Gucci's answer under my direction was bold, sexual, provocative — desire through confrontation. Hermès's answer has always been something entirely different: desire through restraint.

Both strategies can work. But they produce fundamentally different brand architectures. What Gucci has is momentum — it must constantly reinvent itself, constantly produce the next thing that makes people want it. What Hermès has is permanence — the next thing it needs to produce is the same thing it has always produced, only slightly more carefully made.

I have spent my career in the first camp and I admire the second camp enormously. It is harder to be Hermès than it is to be Gucci, not because Hermès is more complex, but because permanence requires a conviction that is genuinely rare in the commercial world: the conviction that what you are doing now is already the right thing, and that no trend, no pressure, no acquisition offer should change that.

The identity score of 9 rather than 10 is my only substantive critique: Hermès has not yet found a way to tell the human stories inside its walls — the artisans, the craftspeople, the ateliers — in a way that would deepen emotional connection with the next generation of consumers who want to know not just *what* a brand makes but *who* makes it and *why*. This is the one territory where Hermès's reticence may eventually cost it something.

**Judge Verdict**: The discipline of restraint as competitive advantage — the most underrated strategy in luxury, executed at its highest level. Score: 41/50.
