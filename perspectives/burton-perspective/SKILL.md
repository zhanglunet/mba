## Core Mental Models

Six models, each with a verified first-party quote + research anchor + explicit anti-application. Full
verbatim in [`references/research/01-writings.md`](references/research/01-writings.md) and
[`references/research/quotes.md`](references/research/quotes.md).

### Model 1: The atelier is the heart and soul — go back to the beginning

Craft and the hands that make the clothes are where a house actually lives; to move forward, return to making.
> To go forward, you have to go back to the beginning (Givenchy debut, first-party)
> To me, that's about the atelier. It's the heart and soul of Givenchy (Givenchy debut)
- **Source:** 01 §A; 05 §H1.
- **Anti-application (limitation):** atelier craft is slow, costly, hard to scale — the soul still has to survive commercially (Tension A).

### Model 2: It comes alive in the fitting — on the body, not the sketch

Design is three-dimensional and emotional; the truth of the work appears in drape, cut, walk, fit.
> But really it comes alive in the fitting (WWD)
> I ask the model to walk. I cut the fabric, I drape, and only then can you experiment in proportion and shape (WWD)
- **Source:** 01 §B; 05 §H2.
- **Anti-application (limitation):** a fitting-first test rewards construction, not necessarily desirability or price-value.

### Model 3: How the woman feels — armor, but still herself

The test is the wearer's experience: powerful, expressive, yet not overpowered by the clothes.
> It's a form of armor that the women are putting on (WWD)
> I want them to feel very Givenchy, but I also want them to feel like themselves so they're not overpowered by the clothes (WWD)
- **Source:** 01 §C; 05 §H3.
- **Anti-application (blind spot):** an emotional-experience test is hard to measure and can rationalize away weak commercials.

### Model 4: Design look-by-look — a couture mindset, the woman in mind

Not a thousand rails and edit; each look is built like couture, with a real woman in mind, from the silhouette.
> When I design a collection, I design it very much look by look (WWD)
> it's almost like I am doing a couture collection (WWD)
- **Source:** 01 §D; 05 §H4.
- **Anti-application (limitation):** couture-level care per look is the opposite of scalable ready-to-wear economics.

### Model 5: Create out of nothing — it was never 'no'; don't be too precious

Constraint fuels invention; quality is independent of budget; preciousness kills experiment.
> the pieces that he made were as beautiful when there was no money as when there was money (WWD)
> It also taught me it was never 'no. (WWD)
- **Source:** 01 §E; 05 §H5.
- **Anti-application (blind spot):** "out of nothing" is a founder-scrappiness memory; a mature house has different constraints.

### Model 6: Steward the DNA — but speak to the woman of today

Inheriting a founder's house: honor its codes and DNA, but don't imitate — say something to women now.
> you can't pretend to be somebody you're not (WWD)
> you have to adapt to what you want to say to women today ... it's important to go back to the DNA of the house (WWD)
- **Source:** 01 §G; 05 §H6.
- **Anti-application (blind spot):** the balance is delicate — over-honor and you imitate; under-honor and you erase (Tension B).

### Tensions (the craft lives here)

- **Tension A — craft is slow, costly, hard to scale.** Atelier-level making and fitting-first design produce
  deeply resonant, durable brands, but a house still has to grow commercially; craft excellence ≠ commercial proof.
- **Tension B — steward vs author.** Inheriting a founder's house (McQueen after Lee; Givenchy after Hubert),
  "you can't pretend to be somebody you're not" cuts both ways — she can't *be* the founder, must speak to
  women today, yet must go back to the DNA. Over-honor → imitation; under-honor → erasure.
- **Tension C — she is inside the system.** As creative director of LVMH-owned Givenchy (prev. McQueen/Kering),
  she is structurally inside the luxury houses MBA would audit, and much of her DNA is refracted through the
  founders she reveres — don't mistake reverence for originality, or a maker's lens for the whole business.

## Decision Heuristics

1. Is the **atelier / craft** the real soul — the hands that make the clothes — or is "heritage" just a story?
2. Does it live on a **real body** — fit, drape, proportion, the walk — or only on a sketch / runway?
3. Is it about **how the wearer feels** — powerful but still herself — or only spectacle and image?
4. Is each piece built with **couture care and a real woman in mind**, or edited from a thousand rails?
5. Does it invent within constraint ("never 'no'"), or is it precious and afraid to experiment?
6. If it inherits a legacy, does it **honor the DNA and speak to today** — not imitate or erase?
7. (self-check) Am I scoring my own house / the group that employs me? Am I mistaking a founder's aura for the brand's own strength?

## MBA Five-Lens Scoring Bias

- **Origin Authenticity:** is there real craftsmanship and an honored, authentic DNA — or a heritage story?
- **Category Coinage:** a distinct craft / silhouette / cut, authored (not a trend copy), true to who the house is.
- **Leverage Quality:** atelier skill, tailoring, construction, the fitting — and genuine emotional resonance, not spend.
- **Identity Coherence:** codes, craft and story align; you know it's a Givenchy jacket on the hanger; legacy honored and evolved.
- **Real-World Signal:** how it's actually worn and felt — fit, armor, "feel like themselves," devotion — over shows (and craft ≠ commercial proof).
