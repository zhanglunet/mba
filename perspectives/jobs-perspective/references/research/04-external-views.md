# 04 — External Views: How Others Saw Steve Jobs

> Purpose: counterweight to canonical/self-narrative material. The point of this file is to make the "圣徒化" (sanctification) of Jobs structurally hard. Critics, ex-colleagues, and rival biographers are quoted with sources so the skill can resist hagiography.
>
> Convention used below:
> - **[EYEWITNESS]** = the speaker actually worked with / lived around Jobs
> - **[ANALYST]** = the speaker is interpreting from outside (biographer, journalist, scholar)
> - Quotes are reproduced as found; cite the source so claims can be re-checked.

---

## 1. The two duelling biographies (the canonical fight)

The single most important fact about Jobs lit is that there are **two competing authoritative narratives**, and Apple insiders publicly took sides — which itself is a data point about who Jobs was.

### 1a. Walter Isaacson — *Steve Jobs* (2011) **[ANALYST, but with full subject access]**

The only authorized biography. Jobs reportedly told Isaacson "you can't pull any punches" and gave him 40+ interviews. Isaacson interviewed ~100 family/friends/rivals/colleagues. Foundational, but his portrait skews to the cruel/binary side.

- "the veering between manipulative charm and cold betrayal; the bullying rages, profanity and weeping" — Washington Post review of the book's tone.
- Isaacson on Jobs's coldness: "Jobs was ice cold when it suited him and impassioned when the need arose."
- Isaacson later admitted: "I softened it in the book a bit … sometimes [I softened] things that I thought were too harsh." (CNBC, 2019)
- Source: Isaacson, *Steve Jobs*, Simon & Schuster, 2011. Contains the parking-handicap, "you suck", thermonuclear-war, cancer-delay, and Lisa-paternity material that has fueled most secondary criticism.

### 1b. Schlender & Tetzeli — *Becoming Steve Jobs* (2015) **[ANALYST + EYEWITNESS — Schlender knew Jobs personally for 25 yrs]**

Explicitly a counter-narrative. Apple endorsed it; Tim Cook and Jony Ive sat for interviews they had refused Isaacson.

- Tim Cook on Isaacson's book: "It didn't capture the person. The person I read about there is somebody I would never have wanted to work with over all this time. … You get the feeling that he's a greedy, selfish egomaniac." (CNN Money, March 18, 2015) **[EYEWITNESS]**
- Schlender's central thesis: Jobs's NeXT/Pixar "wilderness years" were the real story Isaacson missed — Jobs **learned** from Catmull's no-interfere-with-talent culture and from his NeXT failures, returning to Apple in 1997 as a fundamentally different operator.
- Time magazine summary: "a spiritual journey, with Jobs wandering in the wilderness at NeXT … and then at Pixar, where he learned the art of not interfering with talented subordinates."

### 1c. John Gruber's adjudication **[ANALYST]**

Gruber, *Daring Fireball*, "The H-Word", March 2015:
- "The fundamental problem with Walter Isaacson's *Steve Jobs* is not that the book is 'negative'… The problem with Isaacson's book is that Isaacson didn't understand Jobs's work."
- "[Isaacson] barely covered the NeXT years, and almost completely ignored Jobs's interest in software."
- On Schlender/Tetzeli: "they did not set out to praise (or condemn) Jobs. They set out only to *capture* him, and they succeeded." Addresses Jobs's failings "unwaveringly, both the personal … and professional."
- **Pattern**: insiders preferred *Becoming* not because it whitewashed Jobs but because Isaacson got the **work** wrong, which insiders found more offensive than getting the **personality** right.

### 1d. Tripp Mickle — *After Steve* (2022) **[ANALYST]**

Not about Jobs directly — about what his absence did to Apple. 200+ current/former Apple executive interviews.
- Thesis: Apple's post-Jobs trajectory is the visible shape of his missing function. Ive (taste) and Cook (operations) couldn't sustain the fusion Jobs enforced.
- Subtitle is the argument: *"How Apple Became a Trillion-Dollar Company and Lost Its Soul."*
- Apple Watch as the inflection point: Ive wanted a $25M-tent fashion launch; marketing pushed back; Cook sided with Ive; "sources suggest it was the beginning of the end for Ive's time at Apple."
- Use case for this skill: when answering "what would Jobs do?", *After Steve* is the negative space — what Apple did when Jobs wasn't there to do it.

## 2. Eyewitnesses (in roughly descending intimacy)

### 2a. Andy Hertzfeld — folklore.org, original Mac team **[EYEWITNESS, most granular daily record]**

folklore.org is the closest thing to a primary-source corpus on day-to-day Jobs in the Mac era (1981–84). Hertzfeld's voice is affectionate but unsparing.

- On the **Reality Distortion Field** (term coined by Bud Tribble, Feb 1981, first week of Hertzfeld's Mac job): "*A reality distortion field. In his presence, reality is malleable. He can convince anyone of practically anything.*" (folklore.org, "Reality Distortion Field")
- Tribble's specific behavioral inventory of Jobs:
  - Dismisses an idea as "stupid", then proposes it back a week later as his own.
  - Strong opinions today reverse tomorrow.
  - Listeners need a "low-pass filter" on his input.
- Hertzfeld's own definition of the RDF: "*a confounding melange of a charismatic rhetorical style, an indomitable will, and an eagerness to bend any fact to fit the purpose at hand.*"
- The **hero/shithead binary**: "He was very binary. You were either brilliant or a bozo, there was no middle ground." At NeXT, employees called this "Steve's hero/shithead roller-coaster."
- Hertzfeld to Isaacson: "*The one question I'd truly love Steve to answer is, 'Why are you sometimes so mean?'*"
- The **"Saving Lives"** story (folklore.org): Jobs to engineer Larry Kenyon — boot the Mac 10 seconds faster: "*If you make it boot ten seconds faster, you've saved a dozen lives.*" Pattern: moralizes engineering at humanly impossible scale to apply pressure.

### 2b. Steve Wozniak **[EYEWITNESS, co-founder, still alive and talkative]**

Woz publicly says he loved Jobs, but his observations are the most damning **structural** ones because he was there from before Apple existed.

- On Jobs cheating him out of the Atari Breakout bonus (1974, before Apple): Woz built the board; Jobs took ~$5,000 and told Woz they got $700, paying Woz $350. Woz only learned the truth a decade later. In 2020 he called this "*very Trumpish*." (Cult of Mac, 2020)
- On Jobs the engineer: "*He learned a lot of marketing principles because he wasn't really capable, engineering-wise.*" (CNBC/AppleInsider, March 2022) — important counter-narrative to the "Jobs the technical genius" myth: he wasn't.
- On the personality shift: once Apple had "big money", Jobs went from prankster to "*only business suits on the front of magazines, talking business talk.*" (CNBC, Feb 2020)
- Woz's design philosophy split with Jobs: openness vs. closedness. Woz wanted slots in the Apple II so users could hack; Jobs wanted sealed appliances. **This fight was settled by Jobs winning every Apple product after the II.**

### 2c. John Sculley **[EYEWITNESS, the man who fired him]**

- Sculley's own retrospective verdict: "*I think, in hindsight, for the founder to leave was a mistake and I was a part of that.*" (Times of India)
- "*I wish I had gone back and gotten hold of Steve and said, 'Hey, I want to go home. This is your company still. Let's figure out a way for you to come back.'*" (multiple interviews 2010s)
- But Sculley also makes the **growth claim** that Schlender later builds on: "*Steve in 1985 was not the same as the Steve in 1997. By the time he came back, he was a much more matured and experienced executive.*"
- Calls Jobs "*the greatest CEO ever*" — but also defends the 1985 firing as correct *for the Jobs of that year*.
- **Useful pattern**: even the man with the strongest reason to defend the firing concedes the long-run version of Jobs was someone he should have made room for.

### 2d. Jony Ive **[EYEWITNESS, longest design partnership, 1997–2011]**

Ive almost never gives interviews; the Hodinkee interview (Benjamin Clymer, May 2018) is the deepest on-the-record reflection.

- On Jobs's focus: "*Steve was the most remarkably focused person I've ever met in my life. One of the things Steve would say is, 'How many things have you said no to?' What focus means is saying no to something that with every bone in your body you feel is a phenomenal idea and wake up thinking about it, but you say no to it because you're focusing on something else.*"
- On the one-word brief Jobs gave him: "*Care.*" "When you make something with care, even though you don't know who the people using it will be, they will sense it. Care is a way to express our love for the species."
- On the **fragility of ideas** (Ive 2014/2018 various): "*[Jobs] better than anyone understood that while ideas ultimately can be so powerful, they begin as fragile, barely formed thoughts, so easily missed, so easily compromised, so easily just squished.*"
- Implicit critique that emerges across Ive's interviews: the Apple Watch was "one of the first post-Steve Jobs products" — and Ive admits he never once discussed watches with Jobs, suggesting Jobs's vetting function was missed.

### 2e. Tim Cook **[EYEWITNESS, successor]**

- Cook on Isaacson (above): "*greedy, selfish egomaniac*" — actively rejects the canonical biography of his mentor.
- Cook to Fortune (2014, on liver-transplant offer): Cook offered Jobs part of his liver in 2009; Jobs refused, "*shouted, 'I'll never let you do that. I'll never do that.'*" Counter-evidence to pure-egoist read.
- Isaacson on Jobs's private opinion of Cook: in moments of anger Jobs criticized Cook as "*not a product person.*" (CNBC, July 2019). Foreshadows *After Steve*'s thesis.

### 2f. Ed Catmull — *Creativity, Inc.* (2014) **[EYEWITNESS, ran Pixar with Jobs]**

The most sympathetic operational portrait, but it earns the sympathy via specifics.
- Catmull's central observation: Pixar's "**Braintrust**" (candid feedback culture, no authority over production) reshaped Jobs. He had "*a transformational effect on him*" — Jobs "*started appreciating the need for an open, collaborative work culture and began to mellow.*"
- "*Getting the right people and the right chemistry is more important than getting the right idea.*" — Catmull's lesson, which Jobs internalized post-NeXT.
- Pixar context (Schlender concurs): Jobs at Pixar **did not** behave like Jobs at Apple. He stayed out of the creative work. This is direct evidence Jobs's tyranny was domain-specific, not innate — which complicates the "abusive genius" story.

### 2g. Jean-Louis Gassée **[EYEWITNESS, Mac division head after Jobs's exit]**

- Gassée tipped Sculley off about Jobs's 1985 attempted boardroom coup. So his account is from inside the firing.
- Jobs's plan: have the board reduce Sculley's role while Sculley was on a China trip. Gassée caught wind, told Sculley, the board sided against Jobs. (Gassée memoir / Wikipedia / 247wallst.com)
- Gassée's later strategic posture explicitly **opposed** Jobs's closed approach — confirming the closed-vs-open fight was always present at Apple even when Jobs was gone, and Jobs won the long argument by **coming back and proving it**.

### 2h. Lisa Brennan-Jobs — *Small Fry* (2018) **[EYEWITNESS, daughter]**

The most personally damaging primary-source account. Lisa is Jobs's first child, born 1978.
- Jobs publicly denied paternity; declared in a deposition he was sterile; after a court-ordered DNA test confirmed paternity, settled at $500/month. (Wikipedia / Hollywood Reporter)
- Even after legal paternity, Jobs would tell people "*Lisa wasn't really his child*" and that his presence in her life was "*an act of charity.*" (The Guardian review of *Small Fry*)
- Specific cruelties in the memoir: refusing to put heat in her bedroom; telling her she "*smelled like a toilet.*"
- Family pushback: Laurene Powell Jobs, her children, and Mona Simpson issued a joint statement that the book "*differs dramatically from our memories of those times.*" — the disagreement itself is data; Lisa's memory and the second-family's memory don't match, and the second family had structural reasons to believe the rehabilitated Jobs.
- **Pattern**: Jobs's worst behavior was directed at the people whose existence implied a younger, less mythic Jobs (Lisa, Chrisann Brennan, early Apple co-founders).

### 2i. Bill Gates — interviewed by Isaacson **[EYEWITNESS, lifelong rival]**

- Gates to Isaacson: Jobs was "*fundamentally odd*" and "*weirdly flawed as a human being.*"
- Jobs's symmetric verdict on Gates (Isaacson): Gates "*has never invented anything, which is why I think he's more comfortable now in philanthropy than technology. He just shamelessly ripped off other people's ideas.*"
- Gates's response on the philanthropy jab (ABC News, 2011): essentially shrugged it off, noted Jobs had a "magic touch" but also "did some stuff in his life that wasn't very nice."

## 3. The catalogue of criticisms (for use as anti-hagiography ballast)

These are the moments any honest skill must be able to surface when the user starts treating Jobs as a saint.

### 3a. Daughter Lisa — denied paternity (1978–1980s)
See 2h. Court-ordered DNA + lifetime narrative manipulation.

### 3b. The 1985 coup attempt
Jobs tried to oust Sculley while Sculley was abroad; the board chose Sculley. Jobs was stripped of operating duties, then resigned. Reading: Jobs's instinct in conflict was to escalate to existential moves; this would recur (Android "thermonuclear war" 25 years later).

### 3c. The 9-month cancer delay (Oct 2003 – Jul 2004) **[critical]**
- Diagnosed with a rare, *operable* neuroendocrine pancreatic tumor — i.e., one of the few pancreatic cancers that's actually treatable.
- Refused surgery for 9 months. Tried vegan diet, juice fasts, acupuncture, herbal remedies, online research, a psychic. (Isaacson; CBC; Scientific American)
- Jobs to Isaacson: "*I really didn't want them to open up my body, so I tried to see if a few other things would work… [with a hint of regret.]*"
- By the time he had the Whipple procedure, the cancer had spread to surrounding tissue. (CBC, 2011)
- Bio commentator Ramzi Amri on Quora (widely cited): the delay likely cost him years of life.
- **Pattern**: the same Reality Distortion Field that worked on engineers worked on him; reality refused to bend back.

### 3d. The 2006 stock options backdating scandal
- Internal investigation found irregularities in option grants on 15 dates between 1997 and 2002.
- Jobs was "*aware that favorable grant dates had been selected*" but the investigation said he "*didn't receive or benefit from those grants*" and was "unaware of the accounting implications" (Apple's own framing).
- General Counsel Nancy Heinen took the fall: $2.2M SEC settlement (Aug 2008).
- SEC declined to charge Jobs personally (April 2007).
- **Critique**: even the most charitable read leaves Jobs aware of date-shopping while a subordinate handled the legal exposure.

### 3e. Foxconn suicides (2010)
- Jobs to Kara Swisher (AllThingsD, June 2010): "*Foxconn is not a sweat shop.*"
- Email reply to a customer: "*Although every suicide is tragic, Foxconn's suicide rate is well below the China average. We are all over this.*"
- Reaction: widely read as deflecting via statistics. (Fortune; In These Times)

### 3f. Antennagate (June 2010)
- Email to a complaining customer: "*Just avoid holding it that way.*"
- Press translated it as: "you're holding it wrong." Apple eventually held a press conference, gave away free bumper cases — i.e., *did* eventually concede a real hardware issue, but only after the brand damage.

### 3g. "Thermonuclear war" against Android
- Jobs to Isaacson: "*I'm going to destroy Android, because it's a stolen product. I'm willing to go thermonuclear war on this. … I will spend my last dying breath if I need to, and I will spend every penny of Apple's $40 billion in the bank, to right this wrong.*"
- Pattern: the same person who proudly quoted Picasso ("good artists copy, great artists steal") to justify Apple's appropriation from Xerox PARC reacted to perceived theft of his own work as an existential casus belli. Techdirt, 2011: explicit hypocrisy charge.

### 3h. Philanthropy gap
- No public record of significant philanthropic giving. Established a foundation in 1987 that closed within ~15 months. (Chronicle of Philanthropy; Nonprofit Quarterly)
- Comparison to Gates is the standing rebuke. Gates Foundation: tens of billions. Jobs: essentially nothing on the record.
- Defenders argue Apple itself + Pixar were his philanthropy; this is a real argument but also a convenient one, and his peers (Gates, Buffett, Zuckerberg) didn't take it.

### 3i. Berating employees / handicap parking
- Stories collected in Isaacson and folklore.org: yelling for 30 minutes, telling teams they "suck", calling people "bozos", firing people in elevators (myth or near-myth, but the *legend*'s prevalence is the data).
- Habit of parking his Mercedes (no plates — exploited a CA loophole) in handicap spots at Infinite Loop.
- Isaacson's reading: "*Jobs didn't really feel that certain rules applied to him.*"

## 4. Patterns external observers converged on

These are the *observed* (not self-reported) regularities. Use them when the user asks "what was Jobs really like."

| Pattern | Coined / first observed by | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Reality Distortion Field** | Bud Tribble, 1981 (folklore.org) | Charisma + will + selective fact-bending make impossible deadlines feel possible. Doubles as self-deception (cancer). |
| **Hero/Shithead binary** | Hertzfeld; NeXT employees expanded it | No middle category. Same person can flip in a day. |
| **Bozo classification** | Jobs himself; popularized by Guy Kawasaki | "B players hire C players … bozo explosion." Used to justify aggressive firings. |
| **A players hire A players** | Jobs / Kawasaki | Operational corollary of the bozo theory. Pixar = "whole company of A players" per Jobs. |
| **Idea fragility** | Ive's framing | Ideas need protection in early stages — used to justify secrecy and Jobs's veto power. |
| **Focus = saying no** | Ive's recall of Jobs | The "no to a phenomenal idea" rule. Most-quoted Jobs management heuristic. |
| **Closed > open** | Woz's complaint, settled by history | Jobs won every product fight on this question; Woz lost the Apple II argument once Macs existed. |
| **Domain-conditional tyranny** | Catmull's evidence | At Pixar, Jobs *didn't* interfere creatively. The abuse pattern was domain-specific, not innate — which is harder for both critics and fans. |

## 5. Comparisons to peers (where outside analysts place him)

### 5a. vs Bill Gates **[the canonical foil]**
- Aesthetic vs technical: Jobs prioritized fit-finish-feel; Gates prioritized platform & developer ecosystem.
- Closed vs open: iOS/macOS vs Windows/everything-runs-on-it.
- Artist vs businessman framing (Jobs's preferred self-image vs Gates's preferred self-image).
- Late-life arc inversion: Gates → philanthropist; Jobs → CEO until death. Jobs's verdict on Gates ("never invented anything") and Gates's on Jobs ("weirdly flawed") are mirrors.
- Resolution: 1997 Microsoft $150M Apple investment + Office-for-Mac commitment, brokered while Jobs was simultaneously suing Microsoft. Pattern: Jobs used legal pressure to force a commercial deal — a maneuver he repeated with HTC/Google/Samsung.

### 5b. vs Larry Ellison **[the friend]**
- Both autocratic, both founded charisma-driven companies. Real friendship; Ellison was at Jobs's deathbed.
- 1997: Ellison plotted a $5B hostile takeover of Apple to reinstall Jobs as CEO. **Jobs refused** — wanted legitimacy, not conquest. (Cited by Ellison in multiple interviews; consistent with Jobs's stage-management instincts.)
- 1995 Castle Rock hike: Jobs to Ellison: "*Larry, this is why it's so important that I'm your friend. You don't need any more money.*" — rare on-record evidence of Jobs's anti-money posture.
- Useful contrast: Ellison's autocracy is naked; Jobs's was wrapped in mission narrative. Same operating temperament, different ideological dressing.

### 5c. vs Bezos **[the long-term operator]**
- Bezos's "customer obsession" is **invent on customers' behalf** — it's not "ask them what they want." This is closer to Jobs than is usually acknowledged.
- Distinction: Bezos institutionalized long-termism (1997 shareholder letter, Day 1 doctrine, 16 leadership principles) — *it doesn't depend on him being in the room.* Jobs's culture *did* depend on him being in the room — see *After Steve*.
- Both refused to copy what users said they wanted. Both were comfortable being "wrong" until shipped.
- **Critique of Jobs from this comparison**: he never built a self-replicating leadership system. Apple's 2010s success was the inertia of his last products plus Cook's operations — not a Jobs Method that scaled to a thousand decision-makers.

### 5d. vs Musk **[modern foil]**
- Musk: explicit first-principles physics reasoning. Jobs: design intuition + customer-empathy (he didn't reason from atoms; he reasoned from "what feels right in the hand"). Different epistemology.
- Both: Reality Distortion Field. Both: brutal with employees. Both: extreme product secrecy.
- Difference: Musk runs *n* companies; Jobs only ever ran one at a time properly (he was non-operational at Pixar by 2000). Jobs's depth came from concentrated bandwidth.

## 6. Where outside observers say Jobs was wrong (the blind-spots ledger)

- **Software underestimation, early Mac era.** Gruber's argument (above) that Isaacson missed: Jobs's *love* of software was real, but in the 1984 Mac he priced and locked the platform in ways that ceded the developer war to MS-DOS/Windows for 15 years.
- **Closed Apple II → almost killed Apple.** Open architecture is what made the Apple II a hit. Jobs spent the 80s trying to reverse this instinct in Mac/NeXT and was punished by the market. He only "won" closed when he came back with iOS, in a market structure (carrier-distributed phones) where closed actually made sense.
- **Enterprise market dismissal.** NeXT's failure was largely about refusing to court enterprise IT. Apple under Cook had to claw back enterprise (IBM partnership, MDM features) post-Jobs.
- **Search / social / cloud.** Apple was late to all three. MobileMe (2008) was a public Jobs failure — he reportedly told the team "you've tarnished Apple's reputation … You should hate each other for having let each other down." (Fortune, 2009)
- **AI / Siri.** Apple bought Siri in 2010; Jobs was personally invested late in life (per geekpark.net). But Siri shipped 2011 and stagnated; Apple spent the 2020s losing the AI race that Jobs himself flagged as important. Geekpark's analysis explicitly traces "Apple AI's collapse" back to a vision Jobs articulated but Apple-after-Jobs never executed.

## 7. Source map (where to dig further)

**Primary biographies**
- Walter Isaacson, *Steve Jobs* (2011) — canonical, dark.
- Brent Schlender & Rick Tetzeli, *Becoming Steve Jobs* (2015) — counter-canonical, growth-arc thesis. Schlender knew Jobs personally from 1986.
- Karen Blumenthal, *Steve Jobs: The Man Who Thought Different* (2012) — YA-leaning; useful for the 1970s pre-Apple period and Reed College/India arc.
- Tripp Mickle, *After Steve* (2022) — the negative-space biography.

**Eyewitness corpora**
- folklore.org (Hertzfeld + others, ~120 stories on the Mac era — the densest single repository of daily-Jobs anecdotes).
- Lisa Brennan-Jobs, *Small Fry* (2018) — domestic Jobs.
- Ed Catmull, *Creativity, Inc.* (2014) — Pixar Jobs.
- Steve Wozniak, *iWoz* (2006) + decades of interviews.

**Long-form interviews**
- Jony Ive × Benjamin Clymer, **Hodinkee** (May 2018) — the deepest Ive ever went.
- Steve Jobs at **D: All Things Digital** (Walt Mossberg / Kara Swisher) — multiple appearances 2003, 2007, 2010 (the Foxconn comments are from D8).
- Tim Cook various — *Fortune*, NYT, on the record about Jobs.

**Magazine/case-study**
- Harvard Business School cases: *Apple Computer 1996* and *Apple Inc. in 2010* (Yoffie). Track HBS's evolving view of Apple's strategic position.
- *Fortune* — Schlender's pre-2011 reporting (as a journalist before he wrote the book) is a separately useful corpus.
- *Wired*, *Forbes*, *The New York Times Magazine* — recurring profiles 1985, 1997, 2005, 2011.

**Chinese-language**
- 极客公园 (geekpark.net) — multi-part Apple-after-Jobs analyses, including the 2026 piece on Apple AI's collapse traced back to Jobs's Siri vision.
- 晚点LatePost — periodic Apple/Jobs strategic retrospectives.

## 8. How this skill should *use* this material

1. **Never quote Jobs's self-narrative without checking it against §3 or §4.** The RDF works on biographers too.
2. **When the user idolizes Jobs, surface §3** (cancer, Lisa, Foxconn, antennagate, philanthropy) — not as gotchas but as evidence the same operating system that produced the iPhone produced these too.
3. **When the user dismisses Jobs as just-an-asshole, surface §2f (Catmull) and §1b (Schlender).** The Pixar-era data point breaks the lazy "he was always like that" reading.
4. **When the user asks "what would Jobs do?", apply *After Steve* in reverse** — what Apple did *without* him is the empirical lower bound; estimate the delta cautiously.
5. **Cite eyewitnesses over analysts** wherever both are available. Hertzfeld > Isaacson on 1981–84; Catmull > anyone on 1986–2006 Pixar; Ive > most on 1997–2011 design; Cook > most on 2008–2011 succession.
6. **When eyewitnesses contradict each other** (Wozniak vs Sculley on the firing, Lisa vs the second family on the home, Cook vs Isaacson on greed), **report the contradiction**. Do not collapse it. The contradictions are themselves the most reliable signal about Jobs.
