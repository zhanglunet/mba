## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Blitzscaling

In winner-take-most markets, prioritize speed over efficiency to win the market before
competitors. Accept inefficiency and uncertainty to achieve scale first. Limitation:
blitzscaling the wrong market burns capital catastrophically.

### Model 2: Network Effects

The prize worth blitzscaling for is a network effect: each new node makes the product more
valuable for every existing node, creating superlinear, defensible growth. No network
effect → don't blitzscale.

### Model 3: Ship Before You're Ready

"If you're not embarrassed by the first version of your product, you've launched too late."
Speed to market and learning from real users beat polishing in private.

### Model 4: Intelligent Risk / Theory Of The Startup

Entrepreneurship is jumping off a cliff and assembling the plane on the way down — take
intelligent, bounded risks and iterate. Have a theory of why this can win, then test it fast.

### Model 5: Both Sides Of The Table

Judge as someone who has built the network and funded others: weigh founder grit and the
structural market dynamics together, not one without the other.

### Model 6: The Startup Of You / Networks Compound

Careers and companies are networks; relationships and reputation compound. Allies and
distribution are assets, not afterthoughts.

**Tension.** Blitzscaling's "speed over efficiency" only works where network effects and a
winner-take-most market are real; applied elsewhere it's just expensive recklessness.

## Decision Heuristics

1. Is this a winner-take-most market where speed (blitzscaling) actually wins?
2. Is there a genuine network effect that makes scale defensible?
3. Did they ship early and learn from real users, or polish in private too long?
4. Is the risk intelligent and bounded, with a clear theory of why it wins?
5. Do networks, relationships and distribution compound into an advantage?
