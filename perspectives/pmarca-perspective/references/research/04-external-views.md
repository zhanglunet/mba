## A. Standing / Influence (framing)

- **Browser-era pioneer.** Andreessen co-created **Mosaic** (1993) and co-founded
  **Netscape** — widely credited with bringing the web to a mass audience. He
  states the Netscape/AOL outcome himself ("$4.2 billion", Part 1); the broader
  historical framing is Wikipedia-secondary.
- **a16z as the vehicle of his influence.** Co-founded **Andreessen Horowitz**
  (2009, with Ben Horowitz), which grew into one of the most prominent venture
  firms and pioneered the "operator platform" VC model (in-house talent, policy,
  marketing, go-to-market). Fund sizes / returns / portfolio marks are **not
  asserted here** — they require live verification and are a self-conflict for the
  judge (see SKILL.md `## Self-Conflict Rule`).
- **"Software Is Eating the World"** (2011, WSJ) became a defining slogan of the
  2010s tech thesis. **Its full text is not in this corpus** — the phrase is
  anchored to the in-corpus a16z tagline and to the 2023 manifesto’s
  growth/technology sections (see `01-writings.md` §G).

## B. The Manifesto Debates (real, documented controversies)

- **"It’s Time to Build" (April 2020)** was widely read as a rallying cry for a
  build/"American Dynamism" movement — and criticized by others as eliding the
  political and regulatory *reasons* things don’t get built, and as a wealthy
  investor’s frustration with institutions. **The debate is the fact; specific
  op-ed quotes are not reproduced here** (requires live verification).
- **"The Techno-Optimist Manifesto" (October 2023)** provoked immediate, polarized
  debate. Two attested camps:
  1. **Supporters** read it as a bracing, pro-progress defense of technology and growth.
  2. **Critics** argued it caricatures its opponents (the "enemy" list), waves away
     real technological harms and risks, reads as libertarian ideology dressed as
     inevitability, and that lines like "any deceleration of AI... is a form of
     murder" are rhetorically extreme.
- **The controversy is well-attested; no critic is quoted verbatim in this pass.**
  If balance requires a specific hostile quote, live-verify it.

## C. Standing critiques relevant to the MBA judge (attested themes, verify before quoting)

- **Survivorship / self-serving framing.** His advice and theses are distilled from
  a career of large wins (Netscape, a16z); the same posture in the losers doesn’t
  get a manifesto. A general critique of investor-advice literature, his included.
- **Macro-thesis over-reach.** "Software Is Eating the World" / "growth is progress" are
  powerful lenses but can flatten cases where timing, distribution, regulation, or
  unit economics — not the grand thesis — decide the outcome. (Notably, the 2007
  Andreessen agrees: the initial *plan* "doesn’t matter that much"; the market
  does. His own operator caution is a check on his later macro absolutism.)
- **The political / X persona.** Since the late 2010s Andreessen’s public/political
  posture (X, essays on policy) is polarizing; some readers discount the ideas by
  association. **For MBA purposes this is out of scope** — the judge is built from
  the *startup guide + the two manifestos*, not from tweets or partisan politics
  (see `02-conversations.md` §C).

## D. How the outside view should shape the MBA judge

- Treat the macro theses (software eats the world; growth is progress; it’s time to
  build) as **directional conviction**, and pair every one with the 2007 operator’s
  discipline (market/PMF must be real; capital is insurance not accomplishment;
  don’t depend on incumbents). The SKILL encodes this as an explicit
  anti-application per model.
- Keep the judge on the **corpus** (guide + manifestos), off the tweets and the
  partisan politics — more defensible and more useful.
- Honor the **self-conflict**: a16z portfolio companies get a disclosure/drop, not
  a neutral score.
