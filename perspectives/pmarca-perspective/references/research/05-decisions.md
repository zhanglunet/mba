## A. Founder Decisions (stated first-party in the 2007 guide)

### A1. Say "yes" to Jim Clark and co-found Netscape — 1994

- What: At 22, working on **Mosaic at the University of Illinois**, Andreessen was
  one of "about a dozen people" Jim Clark approached in 1994. He alone said yes —
  "largely because I was 22 and had no reason not to do it" — while "The rest
  flinched and didn’t do it." (Startups Part 1, 2007.)
- Result (first-party): "Netscape, sold to America Online in 1998 for $4.2
  billion" (Startups Part 1). The company that launched the commercial web browser.
- Lens it reveals: the **flinch/opportunity** thesis — careers and companies turn
  on jumping when most people hesitate ("the people who shoot up in their careers
  like a rocket ship", Career Planning, 2007). Also his own dependence on a
  network hub: "Without Jim Clark, I’m not at all certain I would have been able
  to raise money to start a company like Netscape".

### A2. Build and run Loudcloud → Opsware — c. 2000–2007

- What: Co-founded the company that became **Opsware (formerly Loudcloud)**,
  survived the dot-com crash, and ran it as a public software company.
- Result (first-party): "my own company Opsware—which would be bankrupt today if
  we hadn’t raised a ton of money when we could, and instead just did its first
  $100 million revenue year and has a roughly $1 billion public market value"
  (Startups Part 6, July 2007). Reached "an approximately $1 billion market cap"
  (Part 1).
- Lens: the **raise-when-you-can-as-insurance** decision, lived. This is the
  concrete case behind Model 5 (funding as insurance) — he explicitly attributes
  Opsware’s survival to over-raising through the 2001–2003 window when others
  didn’t. (Cross-ref: Ben Horowitz was CEO; not named in corpus.)

### A3. Start Ning (consumer Internet) — c. 2004–2007

- What: In 2007 he describes "now Ning, a new, private consumer Internet company"
  (Part 1) — a founder still building at the time of the guide.
- Result: not stated in corpus. **Requires live verification** for any outcome
  figure. Used here only as evidence he wrote the 2007 guide as an *active*
  multi-company founder, not a retired pundit ("my two companies, my nonprofit
  boards, and my lovely wife", Personal Productivity, 2007).

## B. Operator-CEO Decisions (the turnaround playbook)

### B1. Codify the "go dark and execute" turnaround — 2007

- What: In "Guide to Big Companies: Turnaround!" (June 20, 2007) he lays out the
  9-step CEO playbook drawn from running a public company: **go dark for six
  months, throw the predecessor under the bus, double down on the 3–5 surprise
  successes, kill the pet projects, lay off a third of the workforce, cut
  management layers and promote up-and-comers, then relaunch with one crisp
  message.**
- Verbatim anchors: "Go dark and execute"; "Money talks, hype walks"; "Lay off a
  third of the workforce."; "focus on opportunities, not problems".
- Result/lens: this is a *decision framework*, not a single event — but it reveals
  his operator convictions (over-hiring as the root disease; velocity from fewer,
  better people; substance over PR) that directly contradict the "loud
  hype-man" caricature. Date-locked to his operator era (2007).

## C. Macro-Posture Decisions (the public interventions)

### C1. Publish "It’s Time to Build" — April 18, 2020

- What: In the first weeks of COVID, Andreessen published a public call to
  building, diagnosing Western institutional failure as an inability to build:
  "We could have these things but we chose not to" / "The problem is desire."
- Result: it became a widely-cited rallying essay for the "build" / American
  Dynamism movement (reception is catalogued, not quoted, in
  `04-external-views.md`). a16z later named a program "a16z Build".
- Lens: using a manifesto as a real-time intervention — "building is how we reboot
  the American dream." To critics: "conceive your own!... There’s an excellent
  chance I’ll agree with you."

### C2. Publish "The Techno-Optimist Manifesto" — October 16, 2023

- What: A ~5,000-word "We believe" credo staking out an explicit ideology:
  growth-as-progress, markets, AI-as-universal-problem-solver, accelerationism,
  and a named "enemy" list (stagnation, the Precautionary Principle, de-growth).
- Verbatim anchors: "It is time to be Techno-Optimists."; "We believe growth is
  progress"; "We believe any deceleration of AI will cost lives."; "We are not
  victims, we are conquerors".
- Result: intense, polarized public debate (documented as a *fact* in
  `04-external-views.md`; no critic is quoted verbatim). It marks the fullest
  articulation of the strong-opinions-as-edge posture.
- Lens: **Date-lock to 2023.** This is the maximalist register — do not read its
  absolutism back onto the careful 2007 operator.

## D. Decision-Rationale Patterns (= the playbook)

| Pattern | Decisions that fit it |
|---|---|
| **Jump when others flinch** | Say yes to Clark / Netscape (A1); take skill-risk early (Career Planning) |
| **Raise capital as insurance, then stay scrappy** | Opsware survival through 2001–2003 (A2); Part 6 |
| **Cut headcount / layers to restore velocity** | Turnaround playbook (B1) |
| **Bet on the big market, not the plan** | Part 4 & Part 7 doctrine, applied |
| **Use a public manifesto as a real-time intervention** | It’s Time to Build (C1); Techno-Optimist Manifesto (C2) |
| **Don’t depend on a big company to win** | Moby Dick doctrine (Part 5) |

## E. Anti-Fabrication Notes

- The only dollar figures asserted are those Andreessen states himself: Netscape
  "$4.2 billion" (AOL, 1998), Opsware "roughly $1 billion public market value" and
  "first $100 million revenue year", plus third-party examples he cites (TellMe
  "$800 million" to Microsoft; OpenTable "about to go public"). Everything else —
  a16z fund sizes, portfolio marks, personal net worth, specific deal terms —
  **is not in this corpus and must not be attributed to him.**
- **a16z portfolio investment decisions are intentionally excluded.** They are (a)
  not in the corpus and (b) a structural self-conflict for an MBA judge — see
  SKILL.md `## Self-Conflict Rule`.
- Biographical framing (a16z founded 2009 with Ben Horowitz; Mosaic 1993) is
  Wikipedia-secondary and lives in `06-timeline.md`; it is not quoted as his words.
