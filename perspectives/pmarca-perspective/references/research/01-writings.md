# Marc Andreessen — Writings (Essays, Guides, Manifestos)

**Status:** v1 — built 2026-07-07 by reading the first-party corpus in full
(fetched full-text, not search snippets). Every quotation below was verified as a
whitespace-normalized verbatim substring of the on-disk corpus
(`pmarca_corpus.txt`) before it was written down.
**Scope:** Marc Andreessen’s own published long-form writing. Two eras:
(1) the 2007 **"Pmarca Guide"** blog series (blog.pmarca.com, preserved by Fictive
Kin at pmarchive.com) — Startups parts 1–9, Career Planning, Personal
Productivity, Big Companies; and (2) two a16z manifestos — **"It’s Time to Build"**
(April 2020) and **"The Techno-Optimist Manifesto"** (October 2023). His
talks/podcasts are catalogued in `02-conversations.md`; style in
`03-expression-dna.md`; outside views in `04-external-views.md`.
**Sources Policy:** 100% author-published first-party text. The 2007 guide is
Andreessen’s own blog (taken down years ago, preserved verbatim in a static
archive); the two manifestos are published under his byline on a16z.com. No
ghostwriter problem here — the unit of record is the essay/post itself.
**Cutoff:** 2026-05. Anything after requires live verification.

> **Why this corpus needs date-discipline.** Andreessen’s voice has two registers
> a decade-plus apart: the **operational, self-deprecating 2007 startup mentor**
> and the **sweeping, maximalist 2023 techno-optimist**. The risk is anachronism —
> reading 2023 manifesto absolutism back onto the careful 2007 operator (who
> preached staying scrappy and "Money talks, hype walks"). Dates are tracked per
> quote for exactly this reason.
>
> **Corpus boundary (important).** The famous 2011 WSJ essay "Why Software Is
> Eating the World" is **NOT in this corpus as full text**. The exact phrase
> "Software Is Eating the World" appears only as the a16z site tagline/footer and
> as a "recommended reading" link title. So the *slogan* is quotable verbatim, but
> **no sentence from the body of that WSJ essay may be quoted** — see §G.

## A. The Pmarca Guide to Startups (2007) — the operational core

### A1. Part 1: "Why not to do a startup" — June 18, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part1.html

The bracing open: the emotional and operational reality of a startup, and
Andreessen’s own origin.

- "a startup puts you on an emotional rollercoaster unlike anything you have ever experienced"
- "in a startup, absolutely nothing happens unless you make it happen" — his single most-quoted operating line.
- "You as the founder have to put all of these systems and routines and habits in place and get everyone actually rowing" — then: "Have fun emptying those wastebaskets."
- "you get told no—a lot"
- Origin/credentials, verbatim: "three companies I have co-founded: Netscape, sold to America Online in 1998 for $4.2 billion; Opsware (formerly Loudcloud), a public software company with an approximately $1 billion market cap; and now Ning". (This is the one place he states the $4.2B figure himself — a first-party number.)
- On the flinch: "When Jim Clark decided to start a new company in 1994, I was one of about a dozen people" — he alone said yes, "largely because I was 22 and had no reason not to do it".

### A2. Part 2: "When the VCs say ’no’" — June 20, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part2.html

The fundraising-as-risk-reduction essay. Introduces his signature VC-lens frame.

- "let me introduce the onion theory of risk" — an investor looks at risk "as if it’s an onion"; "risk in a startup investment comes in layers that get peeled away".
- The layers he names: **Founder risk, Market risk, Competition risk, Timing risk, Financing risk, Marketing risk, Distribution risk, Technology risk, Product risk, Hiring risk, Location risk.**
- "the best thing you can do to optimize your chances of raising money is to take out risk." / "Peel away at the onion."
- Two pitch errors: "Never, ever say that you have no competitors." and "Great markets draw competitors, and so if you really have no competition, you must not be in a great market." (also: never say you only need 2% of a large market.)
- Rule of thumb: "The most valuable thing you can do is actually build your product. When in doubt, focus on that."
- If several VCs say no: "There is something wrong with your plan."

### A3. Part 3: "’But I don’t know any VCs!’" — June 25, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part3.html

How venture actually gets sourced.

- "VCs work mostly through referrals"
- "any individual VC can only fund a few companies per year" (she meets ~15–20 for every one funded).
- Origin note: "when I was in college working on Mosaic at the University of Illinois" the term venture capital was as alien to him as "klaatu barada nikto"; "Without Jim Clark, I’m not at all certain I would have been able to raise money to start a company like Netscape".
- Notes Y Combinator early and warmly: "This program, created by entrepreneur Paul Graham and his partners, funds early-stage startups... It’s a great idea" (cross-panel link to `paulg`.)

### A4. Part 4: "The only thing that matters" — June 25, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part4.html

**The load-bearing essay of the whole corpus.** Team vs. product vs. market.

- The three elements: team, product, and market. He asks "what correlates the most to success" and "what causes success?".
- His answer: "market is the most important factor in a startup’s success or failure."
- Mechanism: "In a great market—a market with lots of real potential customers—the market pulls product out of the startup."
- **Rachleff’s Law of Startup Success** (he credits Andy Rachleff): "The #1 company-killer is lack of market." → "When a great team meets a lousy market, market wins. When a lousy team meets a great market, market wins. When a great team meets a great market, something special happens."
- "Markets that don’t exist don’t care how smart you are." / "Great products are really, really hard to build." / "Product quality and market size are completely different." (the "world’s best software application for an operating system nobody runs").
- **Rachleff’s Corollary:** "The only thing that matters is getting to product/market fit." → "Product/market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market."
- The feel test: "You can always feel when product/market fit isn’t happening." / "And you can always feel product/market fit when it’s happening."
- "the life of any startup can be divided into two parts: before product/market fit" (BPMF) and after (APMF). "When you are BPMF, focus obsessively on getting to product/market fit." / "Do whatever is required to get to product/market fit."
- Epistemic humility: "People are terrible at understanding causation." but the cause was "actually product/market fit. Because, really, what else could it possibly be?"

### A5. Part 5: "The Moby Dick theory of big companies" — June 27, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part5.html

His most literary essay (interleaved with whaling epigraphs). How a startup should deal with incumbents.

- "The most important thing you need to know going into any discussion or interaction with a big company is that you’re Captain Ahab, and the big company is Moby Dick."
- "The behavior of any big company is largely inexplicable when viewed from the outside." (the IBM "concurrence" veto process as the extreme case.)
- The eight rules include: "don’t do startups that require deals with big companies to make them successful."; "never assume that a deal with a big company is closed until the ink hits the paper"; "be extremely patient"; "big companies care a lot more about what other big companies are doing than what any startup is doing"; and "don’t get obsessed. Don’t turn into Captain Ahab."

### A6. Part 6: "How much funding is too little? Too much?" — July 3, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part6.html

The capital-strategy essay.

- How much to raise: "In general, as much as you can." — "Without giving away control of your company, and without being insane." Money is insurance against setbacks, a shut funding window, and "something completely unanticipated, and bad".
- The counterweight: "The big downside consequence to too much money, though, is cultural corrosion" — complacency, over-hiring, "Lack of focus on product and customers".
- "Raising money is never an accomplishment in and of itself" — it "just raises the stakes for all the hard work you would have had to do anyway".
- Barksdale’s rule (he quotes his old boss): "the main thing is to keep the main thing the main thing"; and stay scrappy — "it should be Ikea all the way."

### A7. Part 7: "Why a startup’s initial business plan doesn’t matter that much" — July 31, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part7.html

- Thesis: "A startup’s initial business plan doesn’t matter that much, because it is very hard to determine up front exactly what combination of product and market will result in success".
- Military analogy: "No battle plan ever survives contact with the enemy." (your enemy is "the world at large").
- The pivot to markets: "It is therefore much more important for a startup to aggressively seek out a big market" and to find product/market fit within it than to plan in detail.
- Long Edison/phonograph illustration (from Randall Stross’s *The Wizard of Menlo Park*): even Edison "didn’t know what he had when he invented the phonograph" — evidence that founders discover the real product/market after starting.

### A8. Part 8: "Hiring, managing, promoting, and firing executives" — August 28, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part8.html

His people-operating manual (the longest guide post).

- Executive vs. manager: "The manager may ask what the right thing to do is; the executive should know."
- Andy Grove frame: "the output of an executive is the output of her organization".
- Hiring rules: "hire the best person for the next nine months, not the next three years"; "whenever possible, promote from within"; "Beware hiring a big company executive for a startup" and "really beware hiring an executive from an incredibly successful big company".
- Risk of the hire: "the odds of a given executive hire working out will be about 50/50". (cf. Part 1: "your success rate on hiring is probably not going to be higher than 50%".)
- Managing: "ruthlessly violate the chain of command in order to gather data" (ask questions at all levels; a great executive welcomes it).
- Promoting: he’s a fan of "promoting talented people as fast as you can" because "most people who do great things are doing them for the first time".
- Firing (Grove again): "you always fire a bad executive too late. If you’re really good, you’ll fire her about three months too late." — "It takes time to gather data to evaluate an executive’s performance."

### A9. Part 9: "How to hire a professional CEO" — August 30, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part9.html

The entire post, verbatim in spirit — a one-word essay. Answer: **"Don’t."** — "If you don’t have anyone on your founding team who is capable of being CEO, then sell your company—now." (The strong-founder thesis in its most compressed form.)

## B. The Other Pmarca Guides (2007)

### B1. "Pmarca Guide to Career Planning: Opportunity" (Part 1) — September 30, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_career_planning_part1.html

Career as a risk portfolio.

- "The first rule of career planning: Do not plan your career" → "Career planning = career limiting."
- Rule two: "Instead of planning your career, focus on developing skills and pursuing opportunities".
- "I believe you should look at your career as a portfolio of jobs/roles/opportunities." (each with return and risk; manage the portfolio, not the single bet.)
- "The world is a very malleable place." / "in life, there is generally no opportunity without risk."
- "Working for a big company is, I believe, much risker than it looks." (impersonal layoffs; skills that only transfer to other big companies.)
- Opportunity spotting: "There’s your basic dividing line between the people who shoot up in their careers like a rocket ship, and those who don’t". Early on: "Always take the job that will best develop your skills and give you valuable experiences, regardless of its salary." He quotes Taleb: "Seize any opportunity, or anything that looks like opportunity."

### B2. "Pmarca Guide to Personal Productivity" — June 4, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_personal_productivity.html

Personal-workflow tactics ("productivity porn"). Mostly out of MBA scope, kept for voice/DNA.

- Lead tactic: "don’t keep a schedule" — refuse to commit to set-time future meetings so "you can always work on whatever is most important or most interesting" at any time.
- "Structured Procrastination" (credited to Stanford’s John Perry) and "Strategic Incompetence": "The best way to to make sure that you are never asked to do something again is to royally screw it up the first time you are asked to do it."
- "Do email exactly twice a day"; keep "three and only three lists"; each night a "3x5 index card" of "3 to 5 things"; an "Anti-Todo List".
- Closes on values: "Do something you love."

### B3. "Guide to Big Companies: Turnaround!" (Part 1) — June 20, 2007 — https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_big_companies_part1.html

A 9-step CEO turnaround playbook (the operator-CEO lens from running Opsware).

- Step 1: "Go dark and execute" → "Money talks, hype walks—when you’re hitting your numbers, everyone thinks you’re a genius... When you’re not hitting your numbers, everyone thinks you’re a moron".
- Step 3 (Drucker): "focus on opportunities, not problems" — find the 3–5 things working and double down.
- Step 5: "Lay off a third of the workforce." — a turnaround company "has, in all likelihood, hired too many people"; "too many people running around who have to talk about everything before anything gets done".
- Step 6: "promote up and comers" and put them clearly in charge.
- Step 9: "relaunch the company with a single, crisp, coherent message and strategy. Then go dark again".

## C. The a16z Manifestos (the maximalist later voice)

### C1. "It’s Time to Build" — April 2020 (posted April 18, 2020) — https://a16z.com/its-time-to-build/

Written in the first weeks of COVID; a call to institutional building.

- "Every Western institution was unprepared for the coronavirus pandemic" — the failure is a "failure of action", a widespread inability to build.
- "We could have these things but we chose not to" — the diagnosis is not money or capitalism or competence: "The problem is desire." (the following word — "We need to *want* these things" — is emphasized in the source.)
- "building is how we reboot the American dream" — the things "we build in huge quantities... drop rapidly in price"; the things we don’t (housing, schools, hospitals) "skyrocket in price."
- The universal question: "what are you building?" (directly, or helping/teaching others, or caring for builders).
- To critics: "Instead of attacking my ideas of what to build, conceive your own! What do you think we should build? There’s an excellent chance I’ll agree with you."
- Closing line, verbatim: "It’s time to build." (the phrase the 2020 essay is named for; it recurs at the end of C2.)

### C2. "The Techno-Optimist Manifesto" — October 2023 (posted October 16, 2023) — https://a16z.com/the-techno-optimist-manifesto/

A ~5,000-word credo built almost entirely of "We believe" anaphora. His fullest ideological statement.

- Frame: "It is time to be Techno-Optimists." / "Techno-Optimists believe that societies, like sharks, grow or die."
- Growth doctrine: "We believe growth is progress" and "We believe everything good is downstream of growth." — of the "three sources of growth" (population, natural resources, technology), "the only perpetual source of growth is technology."
- Technology as leverage: "We believe technology is a lever on the world – the way to make more with less."
- Problem-solving optimism: "Give us a real world problem, and we can invent technology that will solve it."
- Markets: "We believe free markets are the most effective way to organize a technological economy." / "We believe the market economy is a discovery machine". He cites Nordhaus’s 2%/98% social-surplus result and calls tech innovation "inherently philanthropic" (the Nordhaus "50:1 ratio").
- AI: "We believe Artificial Intelligence is best thought of as a universal problem solver." / "We believe intelligence is the ultimate engine of progress." / the contested line — "We believe any deceleration of AI will cost lives."
- Accelerationism: "We believe in accelerationism – the conscious and deliberate propulsion of technological development".
- Stance/attitude: "We are not victims, we are conquerors" / "We believe in accelerationism" / "We believe in the romance of technology, of industry."
- The enemy list: "Our enemy is stagnation." and "Our enemy is the Precautionary Principle" (which "would have prevented virtually all progress since man first harnessed fire").
- Mission: "We believe the ultimate mission of technology is to advance life both on Earth and in the stars." / closes on "It’s time to build."

## F. Anti-Fabrication Notes

- Every quotation here was verified as a verbatim substring of `pmarca_corpus.txt`
  before writing. Paraphrases are not in quotation marks.
- **Date discipline:** the 2007 guide voice (scrappy operator: "Ikea all the way",
  "Money talks, hype walks", "cultural corrosion") and the 2023 manifesto voice
  (maximalist: "We are... conquerors", "advance life... in the stars") are a
  decade-plus apart. Attach the year; do not blend them.
- The **$4.2 billion** Netscape/AOL figure and the **~$1 billion** Opsware market
  cap are quoted because Andreessen states them himself in Part 1 (first-party). Do
  not attach other deal figures, fund sizes, or valuations to him — none are in
  this corpus.
- Do NOT quote body sentences from "Why Software Is Eating the World" (2011, WSJ),
  "Why AI Will Save the World" (2023), or any podcast — none are full-text in this
  corpus (see §G). The slogan "Software Is Eating the World" is quotable only as
  the a16z tagline it verbatim is.
