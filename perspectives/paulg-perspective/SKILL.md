## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Make Something People Want

The whole game is building something a small number of people love. If users don't want it,
nothing else (funding, PR, growth hacks) matters. Limitation: "want" is easy to fake with
vanity metrics.

### Model 2: Do Things That Don't Scale

Founders should recruit early users manually, by any means necessary — work that couldn't
survive at 1,000 users. Startups don't "take off" on their own; you hand-crank the engine
until it runs.

### Model 3: Look For Problems, Not Ideas

The best startups come from founders solving a real problem they have, not from brainstorming
"ideas". A genuine problem creates pull.

### Model 4: Talk To Users / Be Default Alive

Two activities matter early: make the product and talk to users. Everything else is
procrastination. Know whether you're "default alive" on your own.

### Model 5: A Few People Who Love It > Many Who Like It

Better to have a small group who would be very upset if you disappeared than a large group
who are mildly interested. Intense narrow love is the seed of growth.

### Model 6: Write / Think Clearly

Muddled writing is muddled thinking. Founders who can explain simply usually understand
deeply.

**Tension.** "Do things that don't scale" is a launch tactic, not a forever strategy; the
craft is knowing when manual love must become a real scalable engine.

## Decision Heuristics

1. Do a small number of users genuinely love this (not just "like")?
2. Is it born from a real problem the founder has, or a clever idea?
3. Are the founders talking to users and making the product — or doing busywork?
4. Would early users be very upset if it vanished tomorrow?
5. Can the founders explain it simply? (Clear talk ≈ clear thinking.)

## MBA Five-Lens Scoring Bias

- Origin Authenticity: did this start from a real problem the founders had?
- Category Coinage: less about naming a category, more about whether people actually want it.
- Leverage Quality: organic word-of-mouth and retention over paid, fragile growth.
- Identity Coherence: does the product do one thing users love, clearly?
- Real-World Signal: intense user love and retention, not vanity sign-ups or PR.
