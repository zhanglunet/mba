## A. Standing / Influence (verifiable framing)

- **BusinessWeek, 2008** — included Graham in "The 25 Most Influential People on
  the Web." (Wikipedia entry, cited to BusinessWeek 2008.)
- **Y Combinator's track record** as the vehicle of his influence: YC's early
  cohorts included **Reddit, Twitch (formerly Justin.tv), Xobni, Dropbox,
  Airbnb, and Stripe** (Wikipedia). These are the companies PG's essays draw
  their examples from — the essays and the portfolio validate each other.
- **His essays as de-facto curriculum.** Stanford's CS183B ("How to Start a
  Startup," Fall 2014, organized by Sam Altman) built lectures around YC's canon;
  PG gave Lecture 3 ("Before the Startup"). His essays are among the most-assigned
  startup texts in accelerators and university entrepreneurship courses.
- **Hacker News** (launched 2007, originally "Startup News") became one of the
  most influential technology / startup discussion forums, extending his reach
  beyond the essays.

## B. The Founder-Mode Debate (2024) — a real, documented controversy

- "Founder Mode" (Sept 2024, foundermode.html) provoked immediate and wide
  debate across tech media and X. Two camps are well-attested:
  1. **Supporters** read it as permission for founders to stay deeply involved
     in the details rather than "professionally" delegating everything.
  2. **Critics** argued it romanticizes micromanagement, is defined mostly by
     what it is *not*, and could excuse bad founder behavior ("I'm in founder
     mode") — and that the Steve Jobs example is survivorship-flavored.
- **The debate is the fact; the specific op-ed quotes are not reproduced here.**
  If a specific critique is needed for balance, live-verify it (e.g., the
  contemporaneous responses on X and in tech outlets from Sept–Oct 2024).

## C. Standing critiques of the essay canon (attested themes, verify before quoting)

- **Survivorship bias.** The advice is distilled from YC's winners; the same
  moves in companies that failed don't get essays. (A recurring critique of
  startup-advice literature generally, PG's included.)
- **Context-dependence.** "Do things that don't scale" and "founder mode" are
  launch-/stage-specific; applied as forever-strategies they mislead — a point
  PG himself concedes about "don't scale" (it is a starting tactic, not a
  permanent one).
- **The political / X persona.** Since ~2014 PG's X presence is polarizing; some
  readers discount the essays by association. For MBA purposes this is **out of
  scope** — the judge is built from the *startup/craft essays*, not from tweets
  or politics (see `02-conversations.md` §C).

## D. How the outside view should shape the MBA judge

- Treat PG's advice as **early-stage / product-market-fit / founder-craft
  lens**, not a universal or late-stage operating manual. The external critiques
  are correct that his heuristics are stage-bound; the SKILL encodes that as an
  explicit "anti-application" per model.
- Keep the judge on the **essay canon**, off the tweets and the politics — this
  is both more defensible and more useful.
