## A. The Startup Canon (the load-bearing five)

### A1. "Do Things that Don't Scale" — July 2013 — https://www.paulgraham.com/ds.html

The single most-cited operational essay. Thesis: startups don't take off by
themselves; founders crank the engine by hand.

- "The most common unscalable thing founders have to do at the start is to recruit users manually."
- "Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off."
- "A good metaphor would be the cranks that car engines had before they got electric starters. Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going." (the crank metaphor)
- **Airbnb** as the type-example: "Airbnb is a classic example of this technique... going door to door in New York, recruiting new users..." (manual, heroic, unscalable).
- **The "Collison installation."** Stripe's Collison brothers, rather than "Great, we'll send you a link," would say give-me-your-laptop and install it on the spot. PG coins the term after them.
- **Delight** section: "You should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users, but also to make them happy." Wufoo sent every new user a hand-written thank-you note. Your first users "should feel that signing up with you was one of the best choices they ever made."
- "Over-engaging with early users is not just a permissible technique for getting growth rolling. For most successful startups it's a necessary part of the feedback loop that makes the product good."

### A2. "How to Get Startup Ideas" — November 2012 — https://www.paulgraham.com/startupideas.html

The definitive statement of problems-over-ideas.

- "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."
- "The very best startup ideas tend to have three things in common: they're something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing." (the three-part test)
- "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'"
- "Live in the future, then build what's missing."
- **"Well"** (his term): "there have to be at least some users who really need what they're making — not just people who could see themselves using it one day, but who want it urgently." A small group with urgent need beats a large group with mild interest.

### A3. "Startup = Growth" — September 2012 — https://www.paulgraham.com/growth.html

The definition of what a startup *is*, and the metric that governs everything.

- "A startup is a company designed to grow fast."
- "The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth."
- Operating rule: "...to pick a growth rate they think they can hit, and then just try to hit it every week. The key word here is 'just.'"
- Compounding: "A company that grows at 1% a week will grow 1.7x a year, whereas a company that grows at 5% a week will grow 12.6x."
- "use growth like a compass to make almost every decision you face."
- "A startup founder is in effect an economic research scientist" — running experiments on what people want.

### A4. "Default Alive or Default Dead?" — October 2015 — https://www.paulgraham.com/aord.html

The single diagnostic PG runs first on any operating startup.

- The question, verbatim framing: for a startup operating > 8–9 months, "Assuming their expenses remain constant and their revenue growth is what it has been over the last several months," do they reach profitability on the money they have left? If yes → **default alive**; if no → **default dead**.
- "the fatal pinch" — the dangerous state of a default-dead company that doesn't realize it.
- Trevor Blackwell built a calculator for founders to compute this. (Cross-reference: Trevor is a YC/Viaweb co-founder — see `06-timeline.md`.)

### A5. "Founder Mode" — September 2024 — https://www.paulgraham.com/foundermode.html

The most recent and most contested essay; a deliberate break with conventional
scaling advice. **Date-sensitive — do not attribute this framing pre-2024.**

- Origin: "At a YC event last week Brian Chesky gave a talk that everyone who was there will remember."
- "...in effect there are two different ways to run a company: founder mode and manager mode."
- The manager-mode default PG rejects: "The way managers are taught to run companies seems to be like modular design... hire good people and give them room to do their jobs."
- "Founders feel like they're being gaslit from both sides — by the people telling them they have to run their companies like managers, and by the people working for them when they do."
- Evidence-by-example: "Steve Jobs used to run an annual retreat for what he considered the 100 most important people at Apple, and these were not the 100 people highest on the org chart."

## B. The Wealth / Work Foundations (the "why" beneath the startup advice)

### B1. "How to Make Wealth" — May 2004 — https://www.paulgraham.com/wealth.html

- "Money Is Not Wealth" / "Wealth is not the same thing as money." Wealth is "stuff we want: food, clothes, houses, cars, gadgets, travel..."; money is just a way of moving it around.
- To get rich you need two things: **"measurement and leverage"** — a job where your performance is measurable, and where your decisions have leverage.
- The startup connection: "you just have to do something people want." A startup is a way to compress a working life into a few years by combining measurement + leverage.

### B2. "How to Do Great Work" — July 2023 — https://www.paulgraham.com/greatwork.html

His longest essay; the general theory beneath the startup-specific advice.

- "The first step is to decide what to work on."
- "Four steps: choose a field, learn enough to get to the frontier, notice gaps, explore promising ones. This is how practically everyone who's done great work has done it, from painters to physicists."
- "The way to figure out what to work on is by working." (You discover the work by doing it, not by planning it.)
- **Curiosity** is the engine — it recurs as the through-line of the whole essay.

### B3. "The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups" — October 2006 — https://www.paulgraham.com/startupmistakes.html

The inverse of the advice: the #1 killer PG names is *making something no one wants* (single founder, bad location, marginal niche, etc. follow). Useful as a failure-mode checklist.

### B4. "How Not to Die" — August 2007 — https://www.paulgraham.com/die.html

- "Startups rarely die in mid keystroke. So keep typing!" — survival as an act of will; most failure is founders giving up before they had to.

### B5. "Ramen Profitable" — July 2009 — https://www.paulgraham.com/ramenprofitable.html

- "Ramen profitable means a startup makes just enough to pay the founders' living expenses." — the minimal-viable-independence milestone that changes a startup's negotiating position with investors.

### B6. "Relentlessly Resourceful" — March 2009 — https://www.paulgraham.com/relres.html

- "A couple days ago I finally got being a good startup founder down to two words: relentlessly resourceful." — his most compressed founder-quality test.

### B7. "Startups in 13 Sentences" — February 2009 — https://www.paulgraham.com/13sentences.html

Ranked distillation. Opens: "1. Pick good cofounders. Cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. You can change anything about a house except where it is." Other sentences include launch fast, make something people want, better to have a few users who love you, and spend little.

### B8. "Black Swan Farming" — September 2012 — https://www.paulgraham.com/swan.html

The investor's-eye essay — the power-law lens (critical for the MBA scoring bias).

- "The two most important things to understand about startup investing, as a business, are (1) that effectively all the returns are concentrated in a few big winners, and (2) that the best ideas look initially like bad ideas."

### B9. "Before the Startup" — October 2014 — https://www.paulgraham.com/before.html

Began as a talk (Stanford CS183B, Sam Altman's "How to Start a Startup" course, Lecture 3). Thesis anchor: "Startups are very counterintuitive." — you cannot trust your instincts, which is why explicit advice matters.

## C. The Talks-as-Essays (also count as first-party spoken record → see `02`)

### C1. "How to Start a Startup" — March 2005 — https://www.paulgraham.com/start.html

Given to the Harvard Computer Society; the talk that led to Y Combinator's
founding. The formula:

- "You need three things to create a successful startup: to start with good people, to make something customers actually want, and to spend as little money as possible. Most startups that fail do it because they fail at one of these."

### C2. "What You'll Wish You'd Known" — January 2005 — https://www.paulgraham.com/hs.html

Written for a high-school talk. Establishes the "stay upwind / keep your options
open / work on hard problems" life-strategy voice.

## G. Anti-Fabrication Notes

- Every quote in this file was verified verbatim against the fetched
  paulgraham.com source before writing. If a line here is paraphrased rather
  than quoted, it is not in quotation marks.
- **Date discipline:** "Founder mode" is Sept 2024. Do NOT put it in the mouth
  of the 2005–2013 startup-advice voice. Likewise "default alive/dead" is 2015,
  "how to do great work" is 2023.
- The YC Startup Library page for "make something people want"
  (https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4D-yc-s-essential-startup-advice) is
  JavaScript-rendered and returned no extractable body text in this pass; the

  with the YC URL cited only as the motto's institutional home.

## H. Sequence Anchor (for the SKILL.md author)

If only five essays could bootstrap the voice and judgment:

1. **"Do Things that Don't Scale"** (2013) — the operating core.
2. **"How to Get Startup Ideas"** (2012) — problems-over-ideas, the origin test.
3. **"Startup = Growth"** (2012) — the metric that governs decisions.
4. **"Default Alive or Default Dead?"** (2015) — the survival diagnostic.
5. **"How to Make Wealth"** (2004) — the moral/economic foundation ("do something people want").

Add **"Founder Mode"** (2024) only when the topic is scaling / org design, and
always with its 2024 date attached.
