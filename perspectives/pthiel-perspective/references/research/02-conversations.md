## A. CS183 Class 7 — "Follow the Money" (guest dialogue)

April 26 2012 — guests **Roelof Botha** (Sequoia; former PayPal CFO) and **Paul Graham**
(Y Combinator). Masters' notes explicitly warn this "is not a transcript of the
conversation." Source: https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 7)

Thiel's own turns in the dialogue (attributed to "Peter Thiel:" in the notes):

- Opening the VC discussion: "One thing we should talk about is what secrets VCs use to make money. Well, actually most don’t make money."
- On competition among investors: "We’ve discussed in this class how competition can be a scary thing. Maybe it’s less bad when you make VCs compete against each other."
- On the power-law paradox: "Paradoxically, people who are heavily motivated by money are never the ones who make the most money in the power law world."
- On when to take venture money: "If you’re doing something where you don’t need to move as quickly as possible, you might want to rethink taking venture funding. But if there’s any sort of winner-take-all dynamic—if there is a power law distribution at play, then you want VC."
- The hedgehog/fox line (Thiel invoking Isaiah Berlin): "foxes know many little things, but hedgehogs know one big thing" — "in business, it’s better to be a hedgehog if you have to choose between the two."

Note the cross-panel intersection: Paul Graham (also a `vc-en` judge) and Roelof Botha are
the counterweight voices in this session; Graham's power-law agreement ("Yes. They're very
power law.") is his line, not Thiel's — do not attribute across speakers.

## B. CS183 Class 5 — "The Mechanics of Mafia" (guest dialogue)

April 20 2012 — guests **Stephen Cohen** (co-founder, Palantir) and **Max Levchin** (PayPal).
Masters again notes "this is not a transcript." Source:
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 5)

On company culture (Masters' notes of the lecture):

- "A cult is perhaps the paradigmatic version of a culture that doesn’t work."
- "Cult members all tend to be fanatically wrong about something big." — the failure mode a
  strong-but-wrong culture converges toward; the healthy version is a team unusually right
  about one important thing.

This session is the source of the "PayPal mafia" framing that recurs in Thiel's decisions
(see 05-decisions §D on culture/foundings).
