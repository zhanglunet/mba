## A. The 0 → 1 thesis (vertical vs. horizontal progress)

Source: **CS183 Class 1 — "The Challenge of the Future"** (Masters' notes, April 03 2012) —
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 1)

- "Progress comes in two flavors: horizontal/extensive and vertical/intensive."
- "Horizontal or extensive progress basically means copying things that work. In one word, it means simply “globalization.”"
- "Vertical or intensive progress, by contrast, means doing new things." … "Intensive progress involves going from 0 to 1 (not simply the 1 to n of globalization)."
- On why 0→1 can't be taught from case studies: "all successful companies are different; they figured out the 0 to 1 problem in different ways. But all failed companies are the same; they botched the 0 to 1 problem."

The intellectual test and its business version, both from Class 1:
- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"
- "What valuable company is nobody building" — the phrase Thiel returns to across the course.
- The shape of a good answer: "Most people believe in X. But the truth is !X."

## B. Monopoly over competition (value creation, value capture, durability)

Source: **CS183 Class 3 — "Value Systems"** (Masters' notes, April 12 2012) —
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 3)

- The three things great companies do: "Great companies do three things. First, they create value. Second, they are lasting or permanent in a meaningful way. Finally, they capture at least some of the value they create."
- Durability over first-mover: "More important than being the first mover is being the last mover. You have to be durable."
- The core claim: "The really valuable businesses are monopoly businesses. They are the last movers who create value that can be sustained over time instead of being eroded away by competitive forces."
- On perfect competition destroying value capture: "the more intense the competition, the less likely you’ll be able to capture any value at all."
- On big markets (a signature contrarian inversion): "the single business idea that you hear most often is: the bigger the market, the better. That is utterly, totally wrong." … "Maybe it’s much better to find or make a small market, excel, and own it."
- Closing frame: "Last movers build non-commoditized businesses. They are relationship-driven. They create value. They last. And they make money."

## C. Escaping competition + how to own a market (moats)

Source: **CS183 Class 4 — "The Last Mover Advantage"** (Masters' notes, April 15 2012) —
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 4)

- The reframing of capitalism: rather than treating capitalism and perfect competition as synonyms, "a better one frames capitalism and perfect competition as opposites; capitalism is about the accumulation of capital, whereas the world of perfect competition is one in which you can’t make any money."
- The four moats: "For a company to own its market, it must have some combination of brand, scale cost advantages, network effects, or proprietary technology."
- The scaling recipe: "find a small target market, become the best in the world at serving it, take over immediately adjacent markets, widen the aperture of what you’re doing, and capture more and more."
- The failure inversion: "The best way to fail is to invert this recipe by starting big and shrinking."
- The tech-frontier timing insight: "you want to enter the field when you can make the last great development, after which the drawbridge goes up and you have permanent capture."
- The talent test: he poses "Why should the 20 th employee join your company?" — the right answer "has to be that you’re creating some sort of monopoly business."

## D. The power law

Source: **CS183 Class 7 — "Follow the Money"** (Masters' notes, April 26 2012; guest
speakers Roelof Botha and Paul Graham) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 7)

- "In reality you get a power law distribution."
- The Founders Fund example (Masters' notes): "If you look at Founders Fund’s 2005 fund, the best investment ended up being worth about as much as all the rest combined."
- The governing rule: "To a first approximation, a VC portfolio will only make money if your best company investment ends up being worth more than your whole fund"
- Concentration over spray-and-pray: "A better model is to invest in maybe 7 or 8 promising companies from which you think you can get a 10x return."
- Thiel's paradox line (attributed to Peter Thiel in the dialogue): "Paradoxically, people who are heavily motivated by money are never the ones who make the most money in the power law world."

## E. Secrets (the source of 0 → 1 companies)

Source: **CS183 Class 11 — "Secrets"** (Masters' notes, May 11 2012) —
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 11)

- "Secrets are unpopular or unconventional truths."
- The competition thesis, restated as a secret: "Capitalism and competition are antonyms." — described in the notes as "a secret; it is an important truth, and most people disagree with it."
- The meta-secret: "the biggest secret—bigger than monopoly/competition, power law, or distribution—is that there are many important secrets left."
- Why belief matters: "But the people who actually solve hard problems are people who believe in secrets."
- Firm posture: "Founders Fund is more interested in looking for big secrets."
- The Tesla example (kept here as a *company-building* illustration, not politics): "you build a monopoly business if you can start with a brand and build a tech company up from under it."

## F. Definite vs. indefinite optimism

Source: **CS183 Class 13 — "You Are Not A Lottery Ticket"** (Masters' notes, May 20 2012) —
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 13)

- The 2×2 (optimism/pessimism × determinate/indeterminate) is the spine of the lecture.
- The vivid contrast: "In a future of definite optimism, you get underwater cities and cities in space. In a world of indefinite optimism, you get finance."
- On valuing plans: "In an indefinite world, investors will value secret plans at zero. But in a determinate world, robustness of the secret plan is one of the most important metrics."
- "Any company with a good secret plan will always be undervalued in a world where nobody believes in secrets and nobody believes in plans."
- On luck as self-defeating: "Belief in secrets is an effective truth. Belief in luck is an ineffective truth; it will stop you from actually doing things."
- Judgment of the current culture: "definite plans tend to be underrated in today’s startup culture."

## H. Distribution is underrated

Source: **CS183 Class 9 — "If You Build It, Will They Come?"** (Masters' notes, May 04 2012) —
https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 9)

- "Compared to other components that people generally recognize are important, distribution gets the short shift."
- The theme: superior product without deliberate distribution loses; salespeople are effective precisely when their craft is invisible.

## I. Technology and the future — PRIMARY (Thiel's own signed essay)

Source: **"The Education of a Libertarian"** (Cato Unbound, April 13 2009) —
https://www.cato-unbound.org/2009/04/13/peter-thiel/education-libertarian/

> **Scope guard.** This essay is politically charged (its franchise remarks are notorious).
> Per the judge's design, this dossier uses it **only** for its technology/future-orientation
> ideas and deliberately does **not** carry its political content into the persona.

- "By starting a new Internet business, an entrepreneur may create a new world."
- "A better metaphor is that we are in a deadly race between politics and technology."
- "The future of technology is not pre-determined, and we must resist the temptation of technological utopianism"
- "The future will be much better or much worse, but the question of the future remains very open indeed."
- On PayPal's founding vision: "the founding vision of PayPal centered on the creation of a new world currency, free from all government control and dilution".
