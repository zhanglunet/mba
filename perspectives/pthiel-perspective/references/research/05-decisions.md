# Peter Thiel — Decisions (with results + sources)

**Status:** v1 (full tier) — 2026-07-07. Decisions below are reconstructed from the on-disk
corpus (CS183 Masters' notes, 2012; Cato essay, 2009). Corpus lines are quoted verbatim and
labeled semi-first-party; a few dates/outcomes not stated in the corpus are marked
**[secondary — verify]** and would be cross-checked against en.wikipedia.org. Financial
figures are quoted only where the corpus states them; none are invented.

---

## A. PayPal: pivot to money-over-email (summer 1999)

**Decision.** After the failed palm-pilot-beaming idea, PayPal committed to email-based
payments and shared the "secret" fast to win the race.
- Corpus: "This is what PayPal did in the summer of 1999." … "they realized that linking
  money and e-mail together would be powerful." (Class 11, Masters' notes)
- The reasoning is his secrets framework in action: a big-but-not-hard secret others would
  soon find → move fast, tell whom you must.

**Result.** "PayPal sold to eBay for $1.5bn." (Class 7, Masters' notes) — the outcome the
notes themselves cite. Founding-year/company-history specifics beyond the corpus are
**[secondary — verify]**.

**Heuristic extracted:** for a big-but-easy secret with competitors closing, *reveal fast
and grow the exponential curve*; for a big-but-hard secret you alone hold, *stay stealth*.

## B. Not chasing the last credential — the Supreme Court clerkship (early 1990s)

**Decision.** Thiel competed for a Supreme Court clerkship, reached the interview stage with
two Justices, did not get it — and later built PayPal instead.
- Corpus: the friend's line years later — "So, aren’t you glad you didn’t get that Supreme
  Court clerkship?" (Class 4, Masters' notes). Winning "that last competition… would have
  meant a future of more insane competition. And no PayPal."

**Result.** The canonical "escape competition" parable in his own biography: the lost
competition freed him for a monopoly-building path.

**Heuristic extracted:** *don't confuse what is hard (competitive) with what is valuable.*
Stop climbing credential ladders when the prize is just more competition.

## C. Founders Fund — concentration on the power law (2005 fund onward)

**Decision.** Run a concentrated portfolio sized to the power law, not a spray of small bets.
- Corpus: "Founders Fund tries to invest in 7 to 10 companies per fund." (Class 7) and the
  general rule "A better model is to invest in maybe 7 or 8 promising companies from which
  you think you can get a 10x return." (Class 7)
- Governing test: "To a first approximation, a VC portfolio will only make money if your
  best company investment ends up being worth more than your whole fund" (Class 7).

**Result (corpus-stated).** "If you look at Founders Fund’s 2005 fund, the best investment
ended up being worth about as much as all the rest combined." (Class 7) — the power law
realized in his own fund.

**Heuristic extracted:** *ask of every deal, "is there a realistic path where this one stake
is worth more than the whole fund?"* If no, pass.

## D. Naming and running the firm around foundings (Founders Fund)

**Decision.** Build the firm's identity on the thesis that foundings are decisive.
- Corpus: "The insight that foundings are crucial is what is behind the Founders Fund name."
  (Class 6) and "Thiel's law: A startup messed up at its foundation cannot be fixed" (Class 6).

**Result.** A repeatable evaluation lens (get the founding team/alignment right or don't
invest) rather than a stage/sector rule.

**Heuristic extracted:** *diligence the founding — team, equity split, alignment — because
foundational mistakes are unfixable later.*

## E. Reading a "fashion" secret → the Tesla-as-brand bet (mid-2000s)

**Decision.** Instead of avoiding cleantech (which mostly failed), back the company that
turned the sociological truth — cleantech was *fashionable* — into a luxury brand strategy.
- Corpus: "You could start Tesla, which is exactly what Elon Musk did." … "you needed rich
  people to underwrite the research and development required to make cheaper electric cars
  for the mid-market." (Class 11)
- The monopoly logic: "you build a monopoly business if you can start with a brand and build
  a tech company up from under it." (Class 11)

**Result (corpus-stated, as of the 2012 lecture).** "In 2005, Tesla seemed crazy." …
"Seven years later, Tesla has built a fantastic brand. Solyndra has not." (Class 11).
*(Kept strictly as a company-building example; no politics.)*

**Heuristic extracted:** *take the uncomfortable human/sociological truth as the starting
point, not something to ignore; a brand is a code word for monopoly.*

## F. The pro-rata / up-round backtest heuristic (Founders Fund)

**Decision.** Follow a backtested pricing rule against VC herd behavior.
- Corpus: "you should always exercise your pro rata participation rights whenever a smart VC
  was leading a portfolio company’s up round." and, conversely, "you should never increase
  your investment on a flat or down round." (Class 7)

**Result.** Presented as a market inefficiency the fund exploits — most VCs underprice big
up-rounds because they don't believe in the power law.

**Heuristic extracted:** *double down on smart-VC-led up rounds; avoid flat/down rounds —
they usually mean something broke.*

## G. Knowing when to sell — PayPal, 2002

**Decision.** Sell when the definite plan is exhausted, not before.
- Corpus: "PayPal had and executed many good ideas. But by 2002, to be quite honest, it had
  run out of them." … "So there was a certain logic to selling the company." (Class 13)
- The general rule: companies with a live secret plan *shouldn't* sell — "There is no reason
  to stop when you can do so much more." (Class 13)

**Heuristic extracted:** *hold while the secret plan still organizes the next years of work;
selling is rational once the plan is genuinely spent.*

## H. Decision heuristics (distilled — for SKILL.md)

1. **The contrarian question first.** "What important truth do very few people agree with
   you on?" (Class 1) — no good answer, no 0→1 company.
2. **0→1 or 1→n?** Is this creating something new (technology) or copying what works
   (globalization)? (Class 1)
3. **Goldilocks market + moat.** Small enough to own now, with a plan to expand; demand a
   real moat — "brand, scale cost advantages, network effects, or proprietary technology."
   (Class 4)
4. **Power-law test.** Can this single stake return the whole fund? Concentrate in 7–10; do
   not spray 100 bets. (Class 7)
5. **Definite plan over optionality.** Is there a robust secret plan for a specific future,
   or vague diversification? Value the plan, not the optionality. (Class 13)
6. **Get the founding right.** Team, alignment, equity — "A startup messed up at its
   foundation cannot be fixed." (Class 6)
7. **Financing discipline.** Pro-rata into smart-VC-led up rounds; avoid flat/down rounds.
   (Class 7)
