## A. Reputation (neutral framing) — [secondary — verify]

Thiel is widely known as: co-founder of PayPal (the "PayPal mafia" lineage), co-founder of
Palantir, and founder of Founders Fund; an early Facebook investor; and author of *Zero to
One* (2014, with Blake Masters). His public brand in venture is the **contrarian /
monopoly / definite-optimism** thinker — the person who argues that valuable companies
escape competition rather than win it. This framing is uncontroversial and corpus-consistent
(see 01-writings). Specific accolades, net-worth figures, or firm AUM are **[secondary —
verify]** and are not asserted here.

## B. Where the ideas are contested (categories of critique, not quotes)

These are the standard lines of disagreement a `vc-en` panel should weigh against the judge.
They are described, not quoted.

1. **"Monopoly is good" vs. antitrust / welfare economics.** The mainstream economic view
   treats durable monopoly as a source of deadweight loss, higher prices, and slowed
   innovation. Thiel's own notes *acknowledge* this tension — Class 3 lists the cons of
   monopoly ("lower output and charge higher prices… monopoly stifles innovation") before
   arguing the creative-monopoly case. The critique: his "monopoly" is really *temporary
   differentiated advantage*, and calling it monopoly invites both antitrust risk and
   conceptual confusion. **Use this as a counterweight, not a quote.**

2. **Survivorship / small-n.** The frameworks lean on a handful of winners (PayPal,
   Facebook, Google, Apple, Tesla). Thiel concedes the epistemics himself: "the standard
   deviation with a sample size of 1 is infinite" (Class 1). Critics extend this: the
   power-law and last-mover stories are partly *post-hoc* pattern-fitting on outliers.

3. **Definite optimism can become overconfidence.** The insistence on a single "best thing"
   and a robust "secret plan" (Class 13) is powerful for conviction but can rationalize
   ignoring disconfirming data — the same iterative/experimental methods Thiel dismisses
   (A/B testing, "lean") are, to critics, exactly how you avoid betting a company on a wrong
   definite plan. This is a live tension in the material itself.

4. **Contrarianism as a failure mode.** "What important truth do very few people agree with
   you on?" selects for unpopular beliefs — but most unpopular beliefs are simply wrong. The
   filter has no built-in truth check; the discipline (per the judge's own boundary) must be
   that the contrarian belief is *true* and the monopoly *real*, not merely asserted.

5. **Politics colours reception.** Thiel is a politically polarizing public figure. Per this
   judge's design, the **panel deliberately excludes his politics**; but reviewers should
   know that much external commentary about Thiel is about political/personal matters
   irrelevant to the startup-judgment lens. Those sources are **out of scope** here and are
   not used.
