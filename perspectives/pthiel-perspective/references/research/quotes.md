## 0 to 1 (vertical progress)

- "Vertical or intensive progress, by contrast, means doing new things." — CS183 Class 1 (Masters' notes, April 03 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 1)
- "Intensive progress involves going from 0 to 1 (not simply the 1 to n of globalization)." — CS183 Class 1 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "Horizontal or extensive progress basically means copying things that work." — CS183 Class 1 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "all successful companies are different; they figured out the 0 to 1 problem in different ways. But all failed companies are the same; they botched the 0 to 1 problem." — CS183 Class 1 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same

## Monopoly over competition

- "Capitalism and competition are antonyms." — CS183 Class 11 (Masters' notes, May 11 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 11)
- "The really valuable businesses are monopoly businesses. They are the last movers who create value that can be sustained over time instead of being eroded away by competitive forces." — CS183 Class 3 (Masters' notes, April 12 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 3)
- "the bigger the market, the better. That is utterly, totally wrong." — CS183 Class 3 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "Maybe it’s much better to find or make a small market, excel, and own it." — CS183 Class 3 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "a better one frames capitalism and perfect competition as opposites" — CS183 Class 4 (Masters' notes, April 15 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 4)
- "the more intense the competition, the less likely you’ll be able to capture any value at all." — CS183 Class 3 (Masters' notes, 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 3)

## Last mover advantage / moats / durability

- "More important than being the first mover is being the last mover. You have to be durable." — CS183 Class 3 (Masters' notes, April 12 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 3)
- "Last movers build non-commoditized businesses. They are relationship-driven. They create value. They last. And they make money." — CS183 Class 3 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "For a company to own its market, it must have some combination of brand, scale cost advantages, network effects, or proprietary technology." — CS183 Class 4 (Masters' notes, April 15 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 4)
- "you want to enter the field when you can make the last great development, after which the drawbridge goes up and you have permanent capture." — CS183 Class 4 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "find a small target market, become the best in the world at serving it, take over immediately adjacent markets, widen the aperture of what you’re doing, and capture more and more." — CS183 Class 4 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "The best way to fail is to invert this recipe by starting big and shrinking." — CS183 Class 4 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same

## The power law

- "In reality you get a power law distribution." — CS183 Class 7 (Masters' notes, April 26 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 7)
- "To a first approximation, a VC portfolio will only make money if your best company investment ends up being worth more than your whole fund" — CS183 Class 7 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "Paradoxically, people who are heavily motivated by money are never the ones who make the most money in the power law world." — CS183 Class 7, Peter Thiel speaking (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "The power law distribution simply means you have to think hard about where a given company is going to fall on the curve." — CS183 Class 7 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same

## Definite / indefinite optimism

- "In an indefinite world, investors will value secret plans at zero. But in a determinate world, robustness of the secret plan is one of the most important metrics." — CS183 Class 13 (Masters' notes, May 20 2012) — https://blakemasters.com/peter-thiels-cs183-startup (Class 13)
- "Any company with a good secret plan will always be undervalued in a world where nobody believes in secrets and nobody believes in plans." — CS183 Class 13 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "In a future of definite optimism, you get underwater cities and cities in space. In a world of indefinite optimism, you get finance." — CS183 Class 13 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "Belief in secrets is an effective truth. Belief in luck is an ineffective truth; it will stop you from actually doing things." — CS183 Class 13 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
- "definite plans tend to be underrated in today’s startup culture." — CS183 Class 13 (Masters' notes, 2012) — same
