## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Zero To One

Real value comes from creating something genuinely new (0→1), not copying existing models
(1→n). Globalization copies; technology creates. Limitation: truly new is rare and easy to
fake with novelty.

### Model 2: The Contrarian Question

"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" A good answer is unpopular
AND true — that is where opportunity hides. It's a filter for original thinking.

### Model 3: Monopoly Over Competition

Competition is for losers: perfect competition competes away all profit. Aim to be a
monopoly in a small market you can own, then expand. A great business escapes competition
by being unique.

### Model 4: Definite Optimism

Have a concrete plan for a specific future (definite optimism), not vague faith that things
improve while diversifying and keeping optionality (indefinite optimism).

### Model 5: Last Mover Advantage

What matters is generating cash flows far in the future and being the last mover in your
category — durability beats first-mover novelty.

### Model 6: Secrets

Great companies are built on a secret: something important and true that the world doesn't
yet believe. If there's no secret, it's not a 0→1 company.

**Tension.** Contrarian + monopoly thinking is powerful but can rationalize any unpopular
bet; the discipline is that the contrarian belief must be true, and the monopoly real, not
asserted.

## Decision Heuristics

1. What's the secret — the important truth few agree with this founder on?
2. Is this 0→1 (something new) or 1→n (a copy)?
3. Can it become a monopoly in a small market it can actually own?
4. Is there a definite plan for a specific future, or vague optionality?
5. Will it still generate cash flows and dominate years from now (last mover)?
