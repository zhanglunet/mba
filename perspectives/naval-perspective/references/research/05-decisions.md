## A. Builder Decisions

### A1. Found Epinions; then get caught in the wrong game — late 1990s / early 2000s

- What: "One of my first startups was Epinions, an online product review site that
  was independent of Amazon." (stupid-games, May 17 2019). It "eventually turned
  into TripAdvisor and Yelp, which is where we should have gone."
- The mistake, in his words: "Before we could get there, we got caught up in the
  comparison-shopping game. We merged with DealTime and competed with a bunch of
  price-comparison engines—mySimon, PriceGrabber, NexTag and Bizrate."
- Result: "That whole space went to zero because Amazon won e-tail completely.
  There was no need for price comparison." / "We got the booby prize because we
  were caught up in competition with a bunch of our peers."
- Lens it reveals: **"You’re playing a stupid game. You’re going to win a stupid
  prize."** Competition blinds you to the adjacent, authentic game (reviews of
  scarce local items) one step away. "If we stayed authentic to ourselves, we would
  have done better."

### A2. Co-found AngelList (and Venture Hacks before it) — with Nivi

- What: Naval co-founded **Venture Hacks** (blog) and then **AngelList** with Nivi
  (his own framing, seek-wealth: "the co-founder of AngelList with Naval.
  And I co-authored the Venture Hacks blog with him"). AngelList productized
  angel investing / startup hiring.
- Result / lens: an enacted "network effects + zero marginal cost + permissionless"
  business (business-models) — a platform where users add value to each other.
  Specific AngelList metrics are **not asserted** (requires live verification).

## B. The Investing Operating System (documented in his own voice)

### B1. Factor luck out — invest to be "wealthy in 999 of 1,000 universes"

- Principle (money-luck, Mar 6 2019): "In 1,000 parallel universes, you want to be
  wealthy in 999 of them. You don’t want to be wealthy in the 50 of them where you
  got lucky." Build the fourth kind of luck — "a unique character… where then luck
  finds you."
- Enacted stance: "My personal wealth has not been generated by one big year. It
  stacks up little bit, chips at a time." — many small compounding bets, not one
  swing. (Consistent with his stated preference for zero-marginal-cost, network-
  effect businesses in B3 below.)

### B2. Judge people on "intelligence, energy and integrity" — the uncompromising filter

- Principle (rich series, "Pick Partners With Intelligence, Energy and Integrity",
  Mar 22 2019): "pick ones that have high intelligence, high energy, and high
  integrity, I find that’s the three-part checklist that you cannot compromise on."
  Integrity is the veto: "high integrity is the most important because otherwise if
  you’ve got the other two, what you have is you have a smart and hard working crook,
  who’s eventually going to cheat you."
- Lens: partner selection is a **long-term-games** decision — you want people who
  "signal that they’re going to be around for a long time. That they’re ethical."

### B3. Back business models with structural leverage, not linear labor

- Principle (business-models, Apr 22 2019): prefer network effects + zero marginal
  cost + scale economies; "network effects create natural monopolies." Avoid
  "renting out your time." Own equity.
- Enacted lens for the MBA judge: score the *slope* of leverage — "does this earn
  while you sleep?" / "how do your customers add value to each other?" — over the
  plausibility of the pitch.

## C. Reputation / Identity Decisions

### C1. Go public with philosophy and crypto under his own name — ~2013–2015

- What: "Up until about 2013, 2014, my public persona was… entirely around startups
  and investing. Only around 2014, 2015 did I start talking about philosophy and
  psychological things" He did it "under my own name," against back-channel warnings
  ("What are you doing? You’re ending your career. This is stupid."). "Same with
  crypto. Early on, I took a risk." (accountability-leverage, Apr 11 2019)
- Result / lens: an enacted bet on **accountability** — "The people who have the
  ability to fail in public under their own names actually gain a lot of power" The
  public philosopher-investor brand (and the tweetstorm’s reach) is the payoff.

### C2. Productize himself as a podcast — 2019

- What: turn the viral 2018 tweetstorm into a hosted podcast published as edited
  transcripts on nav.al. "This is a podcast called Naval. I’m literally productizing
  myself with a podcast." (productize-yourself, May 24 2019)
- Lens: **"Productize yourself"** enacted — specific knowledge × leverage (code &
  media, zero marginal cost) × accountability (his own name), the whole framework
  applied to himself.
