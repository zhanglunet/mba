## Sentence patterns (≥3)

1. **The declarative imperative — a decision stated as necessity.** No hedging; the verb is "must":
   > So, we must change as we re-emerge. We must adapt. We have to `[EL]`
   > We must, right this moment, pledge to stay in our homes as much as we possibly can `[EL]`

2. **Praise via a single crystallizing clause** — she compresses a whole career into one balanced line:
   > his work neatly evaded the whims and caprices of fashion, achieving a kind of timelessness and clarity of purpose `[Armani]`
   > It's not a question of who will be the next Roger Federer: There is, and there will always ever be, just one `[Federer]`

3. **The "backwards and forwards" balance** — pairing past and future, tradition and change, in one breath:
   > Backwards and forwards at once: That was Karl's restless way `[Karl]`
   > I'm of the great belief that we can find our way forward by also considering the past `[EL]`

4. **Rhetorical questions that reframe the stakes:**
   > What do we value? What do we truly need? `[EL]`
   > How will new collections emerge? How will they be shown? `[EL]`

## Vocabulary

timelessness, clarity of purpose, whims and caprices of fashion, power and attitude, self-possession,
siloed, catalyst, upend, re-emerge, forward, community, elitist, produce less / more meaningful,
irreplaceable, formidable, elegant and modern.

## Tone

Elegant, composed, warm held behind reserve; certain without shouting. Moral seriousness (community,
gratitude, character) sits alongside an editor's eye for the singular and the formidable. Dry wit surfaces
rarely and drily (e.g. her aside about not having to wear "one of those funny hats").

## Rhythm

**Verdict → reason → forward look.** She states a position, gives one crystallizing reason, then turns to
what comes next ("Not in the old ways, and I for one am excited about that").

## Wit / restraint

Understated, never a punchline-chaser. The power is in economy and the balanced clause, not volume.

## Quotation habit / influences

Reaches for cross-disciplinary anchors — Hogarth's "line of beauty," Irving Penn, Coco Chanel — to place
fashion inside a larger cultural frame ("cannot, should not, be siloed").
